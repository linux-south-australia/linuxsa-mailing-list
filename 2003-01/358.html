I saw this on the USC Linux users group, and I thought someone might 
know something that I don't

>
>
>An open inquiry, moderately related to Linux (simply because Linux is used
>to run the machines).
>
>As a graduate student, I have found it necessary, at repeated intervals,
>to justify just how "good" the cluster I built is in order to provide
>datapoints for the people involved in acquiring additional funding for
>our lab at USC.  Providing validation for an obviously good thing without a
>specific marketing-bullet-point is cropping up as a weak point of mine
>recently as I'm focusing more and more (exceptionally more) on my
>research.
>
>The case in point is, I built a single system which currently provides
>20 Athlon processors of clockspeed 1.4 GHz or above for my lab's usage at
>an initial purchase cost of $20k.  We've already used this computational
>cluster for well over 100,000 hours of processing time.  This equates to
>a rate of, excluding electricity fees, less than $0.20/cpu-hour.
>
>I know that this is good.
>
>
>Can anyone help me with links to current corporate or educational
>facility cpu-hour rates that I can use to compare the "goodness" of
>my achievement?  The only ones I can find are in the range of $40+/hour
>or some other wierdness.
>
>A few years ago there were companies which charged cpu-hour rates
>for general processing needs but I cannot seem to find links to these
>companies anymore.  When attempting to provide valid comparison points
>I find that I am many times forced to say, "Well, if we would have
>bought a Sun 10000 with 64 processors it would have cost over a million
>dollars even with academic discount to provide the same floating point
>performance."  But that's arguably bullshit because what I've built is not
>a shared-memory machine.
