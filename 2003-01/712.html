It's also worth keeping in mind other single points of failure that will
be between your users and your web server. Many people seem to have a
round-robin (or smarter) reverse transparent proxy running on a firewall
machine, which passes the request to the next available web server. The
problem is when the firewall goes down. Both web servers are fine and
happy, but could have caught on fire for all your users know. So you
make the firewall redundant (not an easy task), but then you've still
only got one internet link and one ISP, and ....

Also, placing the two redundant servers in the same geographical
location may be unacceptable too (in case of fire, flood, nuclear war,
the cleaners, etc).

It's also worth noting the difference between failover and load
balancing. You may find that one is better than the other for the task
at hand, whereas the other may be completely inappropriate.
