The book is:

-   128 pages
-   h=20.7cm, w=15.0cm, d=0.4cm
-   glossy, washable paper like the cheap supermarket recipe books
-   page 2: full page ad for netcraft
-   pages 4-5: nicely laid-out contents:
    -   intro 7-12
    -   installation 13-16
    -   user guide 27-82
    -   X window system 83-100
    -   admin guide 101-114
    -   resources (i.e. where to get help) 115-120
    -   info 121-128
-   history of computing, unix, linux and gnu: 8-11
-   very clear looking installation guide, including really wind95-similar
    looking screenshots of device manager. In fact, they're
    suggesting that you use wind95/98 to get the config info
    for setting up linux, which seems sensible.
-   the user guide has info and screenshots of some "afterstep"
    window manager and "xearth"
-   there's 5 pages on how to recompile the kernel
-   info on heaps of redhat tools, rpms etc.
-   the "enlightenment 0.14" window manager and screenshots
-   4 pages on gnome
-   how to set up apache server and samba
-   heaps of other stuff

Conclusion:

In the first 10 seconds, my reaction was that I was disappointed
by the small size of this booklet.
But then it occurred to me that it is precisely what is needed for
the beginner, who has proably some idea of how to usewind95/98/nt.

Not everyone wants a 600-page manual filled with detail.
Most people would really prefer to just be told how to
set it up, how to use it, and how to configure it, and later
they'll get into it.
It's a perfect "getting started" book for people who are
not linux experts, and who do not wish to feel 3 months of
pain before they get the thing going.

Conclusion of conclusion:
I recommend this for people who want to try linux
for the first time. It should take a lot of pain out of the
experience.
