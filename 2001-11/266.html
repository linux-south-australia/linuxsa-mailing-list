I think it's quite understandable that web designers do this.  They want
their site to look beautiful, but HTML is not designed to give the
control over layout that they need, so they produce multiple different
versions, each designed to take advantage of 'extended' features offered
in different browsers.  There's the IE4 version; the IE5 version, the
Netscape 4 version, and so on.  Of course they can't provide a unique
version for every browser, and I can sort of understand their difficulty
in deciding whether or not to support a crappy looking generic version
for all of the other browsers.  Personally I think they have the whole
idea of browsers wrong as soon as they start using non-standard features.
It's supposed to be a description of content without a great concern
about layout.  Damn it, what about deaf people?  Or blind people?
Because neither IE5 nor Netscape provide the specialised browsers designed
for differently abled people, such as spoken browsers.  As if a 3 em top
margin makes any different to a spoken rendition of the page!  Good grief.
