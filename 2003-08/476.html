   >  Hmmm. Is there a licence that has the 'good' aspects of both BSD and
   >  GPL? i.e.
   >  
   >  1) Requires source code inclusion with derived products
   >  2) Ensures buyers/users of derived works have the same rights
   >  3) but allows the 'original' [1] author to negotiate a closed source
   >  sale (to a particular vendor for their improvements only - i.e. sell a
   >  BSD style freedom to a party)...
   >  
   >  Perhaps this would be a good compromise...

There's no reason why you can't dual-licence your own code.  You can sell a proprietry closed-source version, GPL the same code base, and sell a BSD release of the same code to a 3rd party if you so wish.  As long as you are careful[1].

Sendmail inc did something like this.  They sell a proprietry closed-source product, and 6 months(?) later, release it as GPL.

[1] Alan Kennington came up with a scenario that you need to be careful of.  If you accept patches from a 3rd party to your code base, you obviously can't re-licence their contribution without their permission.  i.e. if they give you a patch to the GPL version of your code, and you apply it, you can't re-licence that and close-source it without their permission.  The Netscape Public Licence (NPL) was invented to allow Netscape to on-sell community contributions, but I digress.
