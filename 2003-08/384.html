The problem here is that University still isn't sure if it's educating
people, or training them for a job, and this is especially the case in
Computing. No-one argues that the current style of teaching is ideal. They
are under-funded, under-resourced, and resultantly disorganised. However,
the goal, sadly enough, increasingly seems to be grooming people for jobs,
rather than educating them. That is sad.

Uni is supposed to be about educating you so you are capable of
self-training. Once upon a time, people learnt in Pascal or Ada, which are
of little use to anyone, and that was the point. You learn to program, and
then you transfer those skills to whatever language you end up using, as
appropriate. These days, the industry wants graduates to be job-ready, and
know Java, have a Cisco certification and so on, none of which have anything
to do with education, and hence, should not be part of a University degree.
Historically, that was what TAFE was about -- the practical, technical
things. You come out of TAFE ready to work. You come out of Uni ready to
learn. Unfortunately, various pressures have conspired to draw University
and TAFE into the same space. This is not to say TAFE is inferior, by the
way, it's just different. Or it was. Now, it's not. It's almost the same,
but better at it.

This is the point that appears to be missed. The unfortunate thing is that
people and industry are now demanding that a Computer Science degree should
be about getting certifications and learning buzz-word language X, and the
universities are being forced into doing it by our wonderfully long-sighted
government (yay!).

Personally, I think we should take another step forward, and seeing as
Universities are becoming similar to TAFEs and TAFEs similar to
Universities, and both are focussed on training, I think we should create a
new type of education that is actual education, and specifically not
training. Or perhaps we should attempt to distinguish between degrees that
are training degrees, and degrees that are education degrees. Maybe we
already are -- the BSc and BA are actual education, and the rest are
increasingly training for a career, with some education thrown in for good
measure.
