Subject: [ittimor] Portugese Law, not Indonesian Law, applies in Timor Leste



1. The Council of Judges of the High Court has recently decided in
Criminal Offence No 3/2003 by majority decision that Article 3.1 of
UNTAET Regulation No. 1/1999 that the law applicable before 25th of
October 1999 was Portuguese rather than Indonesian law.

2. The Constitution of the Democratic Republic of Timor Leste states
in section 165: "Laws and Regulations in force in East Timor shall
continue to be applicable to all matters to the extent that they are
inconsistent with the Constitution or the principles contained therein".

3. The Council of Judges interpretation of Article 3.1 of UNTAET
Regulation No. 1/1999 is that because the Indonesian occupation of
East Timor has no valid legal recognition, nor does the application of
Indonesian law. 

4. It is the considered opinion of this author that although it is
commonly assumed that the UNTAET Regulation (and the exceptions
granted in section 3.2 and section 3.3) refers to Indonesian law, this
is not explicit in the Regulation and the application of those
exceptions do not contradict their application to Portuguese law.

5. Therefore, unless specifically in contradiction with the
Constitution of Timor-Leste or altered by the National Parliament,
Portuguese law continues to be the valid legal authority. This
apparently includes international conventions such as the Berne
Convention for Literary and Artistic Works and some 23 other treaties
in the field of intellectual property.

6. This has significant implications for the production, distribution
and implementation of computer software throughout the country. As is
well known, the Republic of Indonesia is not a signatory to the Berne
Convention and proprietary software is commonly distributed without
license. This practice has continued in Timor-Leste, throughout
government and non-government organizations.

7. If the Council of Judges interpretation is correct on this matter -
and I reiterate that my considered opinion is that they are - then
government, non-government and individuals are liable for licensing
breaches. By way of example, the list unit cost of the Microsoft XP
(NT 5.1) operating system is $299 USD and the list unit cost of
Microsoft Office XP Professional Edition is $499 USD (official
Microsoft retail pricing).

8. Government, non-government and individual holders of proprietary
software effectively face three choices; (i) purchase licensed copies
of proprietary software (ii) replace the non-licensed software with
freely distributable software or (iii) risk prosecution as per
international treaties on intellectual property.

9. In the Ministry of Foreign Affairs and Cooperation, we hold two
licensed copies of Microsoft Advanced Server 2000. Significant
quantities of other software (approximately 65 units of the Microsoft
Professional XP Operating System and 65 units of the Microsoft Office
XP Professional edition) are non-licensed proprietary software.

10. I strongly recommend against breaching international treaties,
which leaves the Ministry with the options of either purchasing
licensed software or deleting the unlicensed software and replacing
with freely distributable software. 

11. Considering the capacity of the Ministry to purchase licensed
software and the relative capabilities of the different operating
systems and application suites, it is recommended that (i) the two
copies of Microsoft Advanced Server be retained, but not upgraded and
phased out over time and (ii) that proprietary software on client
computers be replaced with free distributions of the Linux operating
system and applications.
