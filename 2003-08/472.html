 > Hmmm. Is there a licence that has the 'good' aspects of both BSD and

 > 1) Requires source code inclusion with derived products
 > 2) Ensures buyers/users of derived works have the same rights
 > 3) but allows the 'original' [1] author to negotiate a closed source 
 > sale (to a particular vendor for their improvements only - i.e. sell a 
 > BSD style freedom to a party)...
 
You've just described the GPL, I'm afraid.

Some compromise! :-)

[
  re: (3) -- The original author, who owns the copyright, is always 
  free to negotiate a non-GPL license with some third party, even if
  other third parties are covered by the GPL.  There's nothing to say
  that a given item of intellectual property can only be covered by
  one license:  you can have difference licenses for different people.

  Note that the copyright owner of -modifications- to GPL software will
  not have the right to relicense their own code, though, because the
  GPL says that any derived work "in whole or part" must be GPL'ed.  
  So I'd be prohibited from, say, releasing a Linux kernel bugfix on
  my web site under the BSD license, even though *I'm* the intellectual
  property owner of the bugfix.  

  The GPL is very friendly to the ideals of the *instigator* of a 
  software project;  But hostile to the intellectual property rights
  of anyone who contributes after the initial public release of the 
  project (unless those individuals collectively subscribe to the 
  ideals of the FSF).

  Just in case anyone is going to assume a profit motive:  The same
  GPL clause prohibits developers from releasing Linux kernel bugfixes
  into the public domain.  You make a choice:  Release under the GPL
  or shut the f**k up.
]
