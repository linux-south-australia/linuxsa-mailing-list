Re: Kernel customisation

Alan Kennington wrote:
> Are you really happy to have a distribution which is incompatible
> with the latest kernel?


Yes.  At home I don't do kernel development.  I use Linux for its
applications and for application development.  I don't need ableeding-edge kernel.   I just want software that works.


>                          I usually keep the same distro on a machine
> for a few years - when I want to try a new distro version, I buy a
> new machine to put it on. But I do like to update old kernels.


Why?  Not meaning to take a swipe, but most people shouldn't care about
running new kernels on old machines, unless you need support for new
hardware, which isn't in your user case.

> Recently I updated the kernel on a SuSE machine which was only a
> couple of months old - and the entire music software stopped working.
> The reason was that they had back-ported the kernel 2.5.x ALSA software
> into the 2.4.x kernel. So now every time I update the kernel modules
> (which is 10 times a day during active deveopment), I have to also
> re-install ALSA over the top of this.


Ok, so you are talking about kernel module development.  I can see why
you'd want to upgrade your kernel often.

> The worst thing is that I can't work out how to build the kernel from
> even the customised sources. When I try to recompile the SuSE kernel
> from > the supplied source package, it just doesn't build a usable
> kernel.> They didn't provide the .config file. So the rest of the source is
> pretty much useless.


Ahhh, Red Hat differ.  They provide all the source, all the .configs for
all architectures, and it's all GPL :-)

> My measure of the "customisation badness" of a distribution would be this
> test:  Can you download any more recent kernel or package from source
> from the net and get it working on the distribution with reasonable
> effort?  If you can't, then you _are_ "locked in".


I see it differently.  I want a distribution that is entirely free - which
means I see Red Hat and Debian as my two main choices.

Using a kernel.org kernel isn't top of my agenda.  A tested, supported,
integrated-with-rest-of-the-environment kernel is what I want, at least at
home.

If I wanted to follow kernel.org kernels, then I'd probably run Debian -
isn't that what most of the kernel hackers use? :-P

> I just mention this as an example of what the article was talking about.
> In an attempt to get more control over users, the distro vendors are
> getting up to all sorts of sneeky tricks to "brand" their "product".
> This kind of alienation of users from the means of distribution will
> lead to much unhappiness in the long run, requiring yet another
> revolution.


Again I see this differently.  I see this as companies trying to make a
buck.  (another Americanism :)  The only way distributors can
differentiate their product from another is by testing, customisation, and
ease of use.

I don't have any problem with distributions doing this, as long as the
result is free software.  I can always work out what's going on, and
change things myself that way, if I have the need or desire.
