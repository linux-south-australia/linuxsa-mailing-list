Which exactly is the same reason why Linux is better than FreeBSD :-)
i.e. one of Linux's main strength is that it *is* encumbered by the GPL.

I'm not advocating one over the over, but rather "horses for courses".

The GPL means that developers wanting their hard efforts to remain an on-going gift to the community have an avenue to achieve that.  But I also recognise that (kernel) developers wanting their code to go into embedded devices may choose to go the BSD route to make it easier for the suits in commerical companies to feel warm and fuzzy.

If I'm developing something myself, I'm more likely to use the GPL, so others can benefit from my gift.  If I'm developing on behalf of a commercial organisation, I might (if I had the choice) use a BSD licence so that the company can build upon my work and not have to release the source[1].

[1] but even then there's a strong dissentive (from a company's perspective) for source to be released (even if it is "infrastructural" and not where the "value-add" is).  I can't see many companies seeing very much benefit of releasing code under a BSD licence as their competitors could freely use it, enhance it, and keep the enhancements "closed".  A great counter example to my own footnote is Apple's "Darwin", but what about "higher-level" layers of software?
