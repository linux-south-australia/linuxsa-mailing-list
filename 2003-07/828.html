I don't see why the source must be open. If someone has a better product 
that is closed, why not use it? The problem ATM is that you can get 
stuck with a closed solution because they lock you in. If you eliminate 
this possibility the open source can compete based on the fact that it 
is a better solution. We shouldn't need our own lock-in mechanism, we 
should only need a level playing field. The ISC/M$ argument is that they 
shouldn't be descriminated against. What I propose doesn't - mandating 
open source does. I fail to see why forcing the government to use 
software that could well be inferior is a good thing. Set up a level 
playing field and then the benefits of OSS should supply the better 
solution. If it isn't better what is the point of using it?

The point I am making here is that the democrats state the following 
argument for mandating open source:

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Dr Chesterfield-Evans said his legislation - modelled on that of Mr 
Gilfillan - would encourage competition.

"I've linked it to the idea of open market competition," he said. "Under 
the National Competition Policy, governments should follow procedures 
that allow competition."
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This argument clearly is invalid in respect to mandating open source - 
you are favouring OSS instead of  closed software. Demanding open 
standards is a much better match for this argument - because it doesn't 
descriminate against closed source, and yet opens the market to competition.
