Michael Haarsma wrote:

> What the hell is it (kscand) and why is it occupying so much CPU time?

It's part of the kernel;  it scans your memory for free pages.  The rate at
which it scans is loosely coupled to the demand for free memory, and hence
is somewhat tied to the amount of RAM in your machine (less RAM will make it
use more CPU;  increasing RAM or decreasing demand for RAM will make it use
less).

If you buy more memory or make squid use less memory then kscand will do
less.  But have a good hard think about whether that's the result you really
want to achieve;  there is a pretty savage cost-benefit analysis outcome for
both of those scenarios.

Err - why not?  111 minutes in five days is roughly 3% of your system.
And your machine is 72% idle -- So why you do care about the CPU utilization
of -any- of your processes anyway?  It's not like CPU cycles are at a
premium on your system.  Web caches are more IO-bound than CPU-bound;
Buying a fast disk subsystem (e.g., a properly tuned RAID array) will
deliver vastly better results than tinkering with a few percentage points of
CPU overhead.

(side note:  I've never quite understood why people obsess over processes
using large percentages of CPU time.  It's almost as if there's a mindset
that says there's something wrong with using 100% of your CPU -- even though
the scheduler does a sterling job of maintaining excellent response times
for load averages up to about 5.00.  You've bought 100% of a CPU, why
shouldn't you use 100% of a CPU?)
