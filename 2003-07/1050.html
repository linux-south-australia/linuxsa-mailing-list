> Mark Newton <newton@atdot.dotat.org> wrote:
> 
>>You can hardly call it philanthorpy when you're saying, "Sure, use
>>this, but if you carry out certain behaviours you'll be obligated
>>to give some of your stuff back to me."
> 
> 
> GPL says, "if you give this, modified or not, to anyone else you have
> to let them give it to me under the same terms that I gave it to you."
> 
> 
>>I've never heard of anyone in a park funded by a philanthropist 
>>being required to share their picnic with the millionaire;  Have
>>you?
> 
> 
> I've never heard of anyone running Linux being required to give CPU time
> to Linus.
> 
> 
>>This is part of the intellectual dishonesty I'm talking about:  GPL
>>proponents claim to be so high-and-mighty about their free gift,
>>but it isn't free and it isn't a gift.
> 
> 
> To say it isn't a gift is insulting to the thousands of people who donate
> fine software to the world.  You should be ashamed of yourself.  It is a
> gift because it is given with no expectation of any reward being returned.
> Were _you_ epected to give back anything when you were given Linux?  No,
> of course you weren't.
> 
> To say GPL software isn't free is splitting the most technical of hairs,
> and the BSD licence suffers from that same technicality.  The difference
> isn't that one carries obligations and the other doesn't.  The difference
> is that one requires you to include certain copyright notices and
> liability disclaimers with all copies that you distribute to third
> parties, and the other requires that you distribute the whole licence,
> not just specific parts of it.  Technically the two licences are very,
> very similar.  As I said before, one promotes the welfare of people who
> will use the software; the other promotes the welfare of those who will
> sell to the people who use the software.
> 
> It's intellectually dishonest to claim that GPL is less free than BSD.
> 
> 
> 
>>>It's guarranteed "free-speech" permanently, even for derived works,
>>
>>The "even for derived works" bit is what grates.  A developer needs to
>>make a choice between "compelled-speech" (which isn't free!) or no speech
>>at all.
> 
> 
> Rubbish!  You aren't compelled to derive your work from a GPLed origin.
> And you aren't excluded from writing a work that is functionally superior
> to a GPLed work.  What you are excluded from doing is taking a GPL work,
> enhancing it, and then redistributing it on different conditions than
> the base work.  GPL forces you to do nothing at all.  It *permits*
> you to do some things, if you so choose.  In fact it permits you to do
> almost anything you like (with GPLed software.)
