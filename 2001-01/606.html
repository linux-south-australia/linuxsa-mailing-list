On Monday, 15 January 2001 at 14:02:09 +1030, Jason Tan wrote:
> On Mon, 15 Jan 2001, James Mclean wrote:
>
>>> Not that I'm saying that programming should be the goal of
>>> K-12 computing, I think understanding should be.  But
>>> understanding thye method and limitations of programming
>>> is part of understanding computing.
>>
>> i believe some basic introductory programming would benifit those planning to go
>> onto a uni computer course, perhaps in C or another easier to use language.
>> which brings the point, that many computer teachers would have no idea, and
>> would need retraining.
>>
>> a while ago there was a plan to get MCSE into high schools, perhaps the
>> governments current plans are leading to this? Would it be hard to get in first,
>> even if only a few schools, with the RHCE instead?? pity it costs $2500.
>>
>>>
>>> Without this understanding of programming, we end up with
>>> a society that is too easily taken in by schemes for
>>> electronic voting or missile defence systems.
>
> Are we talking about computing here or the use of computers in
> schools?

Yes.

> If we are specifically talking about computing, i.e. basic computer
> science, then sure open source will be fine.
>
> But if you are talking about the ordinary every day use of computers in
> schools ( at least in junior primary and primary schools), the usage is
> not much different to business.

I'm not so much concerned about what their intention is.  I'm thinking
more about what should be happening.  By training a generation of
Microsoft operators, we're condemning them to being second-best to
people in other countries--see Richard's comments.

> I.e. they use PCs to do other work.
>
> The PC and associated applications are not the topic themselves,
> they are simply tools, like pencils and books.
>
> At least this is how it is in the school where my mum is k-7 librarian and
> at the schools of her schoolteacher friends.
>
> Under this scenario it is very difficult to see open source being
> particularly useful.
>
> Especially if you think that they might also want to prepare kids for what
> they will find in the real world one day. These days what most people find
> on real world PCs is word/excel/windows.

I hope that in 10 years time better things will be generally
available.  They should be training the kids for the future, not the
present.

> Not only that most teachers know word/excel/windows and have enough
> trouble using it, let alone learning/using another tos, particularly when
> they have little interest in doing so, and probably even if they were
> interested it would take them several years to get to a usable level.
>
> The training supplied by the dept is dismal to non existent.

OK, but we don't have to accept that as a given.

> So while it is possible that they might use star office for their needs,
> it is highly unlikely that it would actually happen.

I don't see any advantage in replacing Microsoft Office with Star
Office.  I'm talking about the individual's relationship with
computers.

> What would be more likely is that the PCs wouldn't get used as much.
> (there are still some classrooms where the PC is never turned on -
> this would likely increase if you took away the OS/apps which they
> have spent lots of time getting to some level of familiarity with
> and told them to start again).

Not if they required it to fulfil the curriculum.

> Remember a lot of teachers don't want to do PC support and don't want to
> do PC training (I don't mean don't want to be trained, I mean don't want to be
> PC trainers). They are not trained, equipped or interested in doing so.
>
> Some do  and I applaud that.
> Some do under the most adverse environments, e.g. no support from
> principals little peer support and few funds, policy made by schools
> councils who have no understanding of IT/computers, completely self
> taught, largely in their own time.
>
> Taking away that investment in time and skills(no matter how pitiful
> these skills are) would be extremely demoralising and quite possibly
> would set computer usage in schools back a few years or more.

So we don't take away that investment, we build on it (possibly
propping up a rickety structure in the process).

> Especially given there is no real practical reason to do so, in the
> majority of cases.
> As I say if you are specially teaching "computing" is computer science/IT
> principals, then sure open source would be extremely useful, possibly
> indispensable once you have it and appropriately skilled teachers in
> place.

Exactly.  Obviously the level of skills would be commensurate with the
fact that it's a high school and not a university, but I don't see why
kids shouldn't learn basic programming skills in school.

> But for non "computing"  every day  desktop use I cant really see open
> source software being all that useful.
>
> There is more here at stake than good philosophy.

Indeed.
