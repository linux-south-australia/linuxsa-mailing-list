What I understand about VLANs is that they allow you to do two things:

1) allocate individual ports on a switch to separate VLANs, so for eg, ports
1-8 look like a single switch, and ports 7-16 look like a separate switch...
so incoming data on port 1 can only go out on ports 2-8, and not on ports
7-16.

2) effectively multiplex a number of VLANs through a single port to another
VLAN-enabled switch. ie, you could have 2 switches, in different buildings,
connected by one piece of fibre or Cat5, and each switch has some ports
allocated to the “Managment” VLAN, and some to the “Lackeys” VLAN, and even
though they share the same physcal network between the switches, they can’t
see each other. Effectively, it’s like having four switches, two in each
building, connected by two pieces of parallel Fibre or Cat5.
