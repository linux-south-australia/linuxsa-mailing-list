>
> Note that the Bill of Rights only binds the Government -- When it says,
> "Congress shall make no law abridging freedom of speech," that doesn't
> prevent a consortium of private companies from getting together and
> defining a technical standard which removes the potential for freedom
> of speech for anyone who happens to use it, nor does it prevent a
> company from entering into a contract with a citizen where the citizen
> voluntarily gives up their rights.  That explains DVD encryption, which
> prevents DVD material from being excerpted in research, and
> legally binding software license agreements where the licensee is
> prohibited from publishing any negative reviews of the product without
> the prior consent of the licensor.

You can voluntarily limit the scope of your rights, but you can never
voluntarily give them up.  These rights are considered "inalienable".  To
say the Bill of Rights "only binds the Government" is technically true, but
somewhat diminishes the actual power of the Bill of Rights.  Remember it's
the government that creates laws that make contracts enforcable.  Therefore,
they cannot make a law that would allow contracts to be enforced which would
infringe on the rights of an individual.

Example:  If the purpose of the "negative review" is to point out a flaw in
the software that could result in physical injury or substantial monetary
losses, enforcing a contract requiring prior consent would be considered
unconstitutional.
