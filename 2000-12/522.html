Re: Cracking down on copycats: enforcement of copyright in Australia.

Mark Newton wrote:
> 
> On Tue, Dec 12, 2000 at 04:54:33PM +1030, Brian Astill wrote:
> 
>  > I don't mind this, however:
>  > "The Committee recommends that a provision be introduced into the
>  > Copyright Act 1968, similar to section 100 of the Copyright, Designs and
>  > Patents Act 1988 (UK), which authorises a copyright owner or their agent
>  > to seize a copy of their work (or other subject matter) that is offered for
>  > sale or hire from a place other than a regular or permanent place of
>  > business."
>  > ie if you are offering to sell copies of M$Office from your home -
>  > watch out!
> 
> I have a huge problem with this.
> 
> If selling MS Office would have the potential to be a life and death
> situation, then I'd agree with you.  But it isn't;  Authorizing a
> company to enter your home without your consent on the suspicion that
> you're infringing their copyright is a remedy which is vastly out of
> proportion to the size of the threat.
> 
> I can see what they want to do here:  they want to allow private search
> and siezure in places shared marketplaces, where people might be selling
> illegal software (trash and treasures, temporary stalls in markets,
> country fairs, etc).
> 
> What they've actually written, though, is that they desire to permit
> copyright owners to enter any building at all which isn't a permanent
> business.  Including your house.
> 
> No way.  If someone like Microsoft suspects that a private citizen is
> selling copies of Windows from their home, a basic sense of reality
> would suggest that permitting them to raid the home is disproportionate
> when other remedies (e.g.: obtaining a restraining order) would achieve
> the same end result.
> 
> Law is a balancing act, where remedies are supposed to be coupled to the
> size of the threat:  You don't execute someone for defaulting on a parking
> ticket;  You don't give someone a fine for murder.  The sanctity of the
> home is a common law right which has been legally recognized for centuries;
> A lot of thought should be given to any attempt to abridge that right,
> especially when said abridgement is being lobbied by multi-billion dollar
> profit-centered companies who couldn't give a rat's arse about your legal
> rights or your privacy.
> 
>    - mark
