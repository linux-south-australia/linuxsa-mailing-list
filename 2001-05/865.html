Telstra ISDN primary rate is physically delivered as a connection 
which supports up to 30 concurrent user sessions. It's actually a 2 
megabit data bearer, split into 30 x 64K user session ("B") channels 
plus one signalling ("D") channel.

It can be bought with 10, 20 or 30 channels 'enabled' by Telstra - 
this is called (surprisingly) OnRamp 10,  OnRamp 20 or OnRamp 30.

In all cases, however, the physical delivery vector is identical - 
i.e. Telstra software-select the number of channels to unblock based 
on the amount of money you agree to pay them.

Beyond 30 channels, you just buy further multiples of 10 channels, 
and they are delivered on the appropriate number of 30 channel 
bearers.

So the answer to your specific questions (in order) are:

	"30"
and
	"One"

Cheers,
Simon

p.s. Obtrivia: Why are they called "B" and "D" channels? Because the 
standard actually specifies A, B, C and D channel types, but in the 
real world, only B and D wound up being used.  They don't stand for 
some words that start with those letters, they're just accidents of 
fate :)
