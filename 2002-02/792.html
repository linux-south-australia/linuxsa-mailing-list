> cards, sound cards, modems, printers, and some peripherals that can take

To paraphrase:
If components in this category work under your Windows system, then they 
should work under Linux.

In general:
- hard disk - if it's IDE/EIDE/ATA or SCSI, it's supported
- monitor - if it's standard RGB CRT or LCD monitor, it's supported
- CD drive - if it's ATAPI/IDE or SCSI, it's supported
- mouse - if it's USB, PS/2, or Microsoft serial, it's supported

Perhaps you could read the manuals or product information for those 
components and determine what protocol they use (if they're IDE or SCSI 
or whatever).

I don't know how things like FireWire (IEEE 1394?) or USB hard disk and 
compact disk drives go.

Further, if you wanted to test whether your peripherals worked without 
repartitioning and formatting your disk drive, there are some so-called 
"live" editions of Linux (for instance, SuSE makes on), that run 
entirely off a CD-ROM.
