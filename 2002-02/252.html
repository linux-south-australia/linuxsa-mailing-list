Re: Fw: DSL comparisons and amusement ...

I do see your point, but people said similar things about electricity and
railway splits and privatisation, both here and abroad.

I'm not saying splitting up monopolies is a bad thing, and i'm not saying
privatisation is a bad thing, but I am making the point that there are wins
and loses, and historically there seem to be more loses than wins.

Toby

On Fri, 8 Feb 2002, Matthew Moyle-Croft wrote:

> > A problem with breaking up one company into several is thus:
> > Multiple privatised companies all try to make a profit. The cumalative
> > profit margin at the end of the line has, in practice, often been higher
> > than before the company was split up.
>
> But the increase in profit isn't necessarily at the expense of existing
> customers.  Often it's due to the size of the market increasing.  An example
> is that interstate bandwidth in Australia is very expensive as Telstra (as
> an example) do not see value in selling excess capacity on their fibre as
> they see it'll take away from their data carriage business.
>
> If Telstra is broken up in the manner I discussed earlier, then that
> component of Telstra is going to be looking for revenue, and thus should
> allow more access to the interstate capacity at a lower cost, hence
> (through the magic of S&D) more people will participate in the market as the
> cost and availablity will improve. (eg. Internode may run their own SDH
> links to Vic/NSW/QLD to allow access to SXC etc).
>
> This means that the profit of that component of Telstra may increase (as the
> extra capacity costs them nothing except Opportunity cost).  The other
> components of the broken up Telstra may experience a decrease in profits
> initially, but as their prices drop the number of people wanting to buy
> affordable interstate data carriage will increase and their profit will rise
> again.
>
> This example is obviously a little simplistic but you, hopefully, see my
> point.
>
> MMC
