Ben Williams wrote:
>>wonder what the 'Bps' in the above means (I always forget whether upper case 'B' is
>>>supposed to mean something different to lower case 'b')
>>
>>In correct usage:
>>b - bit
>>B - byte
>>
>>Kb/s - 1000 bits per second
>>KB/s - 1000 bytes per second
>>KiB/s - 1024 bytes per second
>>
>>http://physics.nist.gov/cuu/Units/prefixes.html
>>
>>Now if only everybody *used* this convention...
>>
> 
> ...and just one reason they don't is, as dan of dansdata.com pointed
> out[1], because kibibytes and mebibytes etc sound like baby talk. you're
> a cute little kibibyte! yes you are! yes you are! gurgle gurgle.

He also uses "b" for byte rather than "B", and excludes the space 
(should be 90 PB rather than 90Pb -- do you write 90 petabytes, or 
90petabytes?).

Why is it that the only currently viable solution (I haven't seen any 
other proposals, and this one has support from IEC, IEEE, and CIPM, so 
it's the closest to a standard we're likely to get) is disregarded 
because a few English geeks are worried they'll lose cred?  If it 
worries people so greatly, why not say "binary kilobytes" or "kibs"?

(I do agree that the words are not the sweetest to the ear, but this 
standard does avoid ambiguity, which (as DansData mentioned) is very 
important as storage sizes become larger.)
