Risk. That's it. Short and simple. The risk is high, and the payoff
uncertain. With MS, the risk is (in most people's minds) low, and the
payoff low but certain. They know what they will get. When talking to
management types, recognise that for the most part, they are
conservative. Give them what they want -- certainty. Make sure that you
make NO hyperbole, back up what you say with studies, perhaps prepare a
5 min presentation or 1 page paper. Make sure it's balanced and looks
it. Don't go into huge depth -- they want the information distilled for
them. But make sure that you quote people who are less obviously biased
than LinuxWorld magazine, or Linus Torvalds :)

Point at examples of companies already doing it. As an EG, the RSPCA in

email, that SYC is Linux in the backend...

yeah, this is annoying. there are two things that people (me inclusive)
tend to do when demoing -- either polish up a demo where they demo
everything that they are 100% confident with, and gloss over the
potential problems, or they try something new for the first time in a
demo, and something doesn't work. Honesty is the best policy. "I don't
know how to do that, but we can give it a go now if you want" is ok, as
long as you're demoing to someone who likes a challenge, and isn't there
for the purposes of criticising. With a critical audience, it's best to
say you'll go find out, go find out, and then show them. In either case,
you have to be honest with yourself and with them. If it's hard or
unintuitive, tell them. If it's simple, but you made a silly booboo and
stuffed it all up, tell them.

OK, my advice is you perhaps need to make a decision as to whether you
are going to be the Linux guru or not. Not to say it's all or nothing,
but in some sense, it is ;)... Basically, there is a LOT to learn with
Linux, especially to set it up properly. If you could get someone to be
the guru for you, then you could do things as you see fit, and slowly
gain confidence and skill without risking anything to the company (the
risk is said consultants'). However, I think it's exceptionally
difficult to try to be a sysadmin as well as something else,
particularly when you are also trying to advocate a new system --
remember the risk thing? You need to reduce the risk factor for the
boss, and one way of doing this is to get a decent (trusted and
respected by you _and_ your boss) consultant to be the fall guy... have
him direct things, set them up right to start off with and put you on
the right tracks, and use him (or her) when you are out of your depth.

Remember though, that the Linux community is diverse, and many are
working on these issues... compare any distro with one from a few
versions back. It's improving rapidly. The bar is lowering, the curve is
flattenning... and yet, the limit remains high. The more people care
about this, and the more get involved in doing something about it, the
better it will get -- and in changing this, it's more important to get
involved than to try to change the amorphous community...

Horses for courses. Competition makes the breed stronger. Choose your
pefferred solution (and often, the most valid choice for someone who
doens't want to go full time, is "I'll have what she's having"). If you
think about it, the car industry could be more efficient if we didn't
have this whole Ford/GM thing. Why not get rid of the Ford V8, and just
go with the small block chev? Surely with that merger, it'd be great --
we wouldn't need to worry if this head fits that block ... of course it
would... :) That said, consolidation occurs, and apt and rpm will grow
closer... and then someone will invent the ACME system (or perhaps
portage)... diversity should be protected and encouraged -- it's what
makes things good.
