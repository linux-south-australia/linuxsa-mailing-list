Are you talking of the second sentence at the (unattributed) third
level of indentation (which I think was David Lloyd)?  Those aren't
hyphens, they're dashes, also called em rules or (in American) em
dashes.  In print they're a continuous horizontal line the width of
the letter m (thus the name).  In ASCII they're represented by two
0x2d (-) characters.  They're used to indicate an abrupt change in the
structure of a sentence.  It's not clear whether that is the case
here: it looks more like the dashes are superfluous.  It is clear that
they are surrounded by white space, which is incorrect.

See the Commonwealth Department of Finance and Administration
Style Manual, 5th edition, sections 6.88 and on, and the Chicago
Manual of Style, 14th edition, sections 5.105 and on.
