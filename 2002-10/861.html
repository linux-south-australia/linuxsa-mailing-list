Re: [OT] Watchdog eyes broadband limits

Quoting davidn@rebel.net.au:

> > The problem is that an ISP will say "download" in their advertisement
> > when they mean "data from the ISP to the customer". As I said before,
> > it's unfortunate that there's not a widely accepted shorthand term
> > for this.
> 
> Ah but there *is* a widely accepted shorthand term.  "Download" is widely
> understood to mean "data from the ISP to the customer."

To those who know what download means, yes. To the user who clicks "download" 
in IE, no.

> > if I need an advanced level of technical knowledge to purchase n 
> > Internet connection from a newbie-targeted ISP there's something wrong.
> 
> Look, it's really quite simple.  People are NOT stupid and neither are
> they guileless.  Some consumers will try anything to avoid paying for what
> they bought.  They know that when they sign up to an ISP the product is
> stuff delivered from the Internet.  To try to claim a mistunderstanding,
> that they genuinely thought web pages, or email, or ICQ, or such, was for
> free is just plain guile.  Darn, already used guile, must buy a thesaurus.
> It's duplicit, artful, dishonest, deceitful, tricky, chicanery.  It's
> almost fraudulent.

Yes, this is true, but it doesn't discount genuine cases of ignorance.

Users trying to suck money out of ISPs because they can't read T&C's is bad. 
ISPs making it clear (which they already might) about what the user is doing in 
their primary advertisements is good.

My beef was with the attitude that a user shouldn't have to contact their geek 
friend in order to buy an internet connection, not that getting compensation 
out of the ISP is a good idea.
