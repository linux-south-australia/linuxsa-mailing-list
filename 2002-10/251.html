Bear in mind that there's more to programming than just programming.
Most programmers that I know of (and to be brutally honest, I'd have to
lump me into this group [1]) suck at documenting, but any process without
documentation makes it hard for non-programmers to get up and running.
There there are people who are wizards at breaking code, and designing
test cases for that (yes, even Testers), people skills at managing and
communicating with other people (is it SmoothWall that has that charismatic
developer on the mailing list?), people with expertise outside of the
computing domain but overlapping with the problem you're trying to solve
(don't come looking my way for accounting skills, there's a good reason
why I pay others to do that for me).

So don't sell yourself short, James.  You're certainly capable of
stringing together a sentence that can get past the DSL radar.  ;-)
