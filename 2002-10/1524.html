Matt Hawkins, Canberra 

As well as the comments made by others, I'd also like to point out these
facts: 

Version 2 of the GPL has been around just over 11 years, and has in fact
been tested in court many times despite Ms Tippet's unfounded claims to the
contrary. 

She also appears to not understand the difference between copyright and
licensing. Copyright is about ownership and protection of the work.
Licensing is about permitting people other than the copyright holder to use
that work. No open source license removes copyright from a software program,
hence the developers of that software are, in fact, protected. 

Also, despite Ms Tippet's claims, assent to an open source license begins,
as it should, when one uses that work. If you run a GPL'd program, you have
agreed to the terms and conditions of the GPL. That's the whole idea of
licensing - if you don't agree to the terms, you are not legally entitled to
use the software. Ms Tippet incorrectly states that the license only takes
effect if one redistributes or modifies the work. 

Another sadly amusing statement was that incorporating GPL'd software into
another work and then selling it was violating the GPL. Ms Tippet should
actually read the GPL before making further false claims about it. 

"When we speak of free software, we are referring to freedom, not price",
says the preamble, which goes on to say that one can "charge for this
service if you wish". If GPL'd software was not able to be sold, then how do
the many Linux distribution vendors get away with doing that very thing? No,
Ms Tippet is once again either grossly negligent or plain lying. 

Finally, another claim "The lack of a liability regime also meant the
distributor was not liable for defects in the product", which others have
spoken about from another view, everyone go and read the two simple
sentences which make up the entire first clause of the GPL. Particularly the
second one, which lays down the very foundation of a liability regime. 

What should be made of this point however is yes, no software engineer has
ever been held liable for their work, and its about time that - like other
engineering fields - it happens. Microsoft has become very rich not only
through criminal means (which is why I cannot run or support their software
- I would be an accessory to their crimes), but through the practise of
charging the consumer for bug fixes (with the marketing spin that its a
completely new version). They're certainly not the only software company
guilty of this, but they're the largest and hence the easiest target. 

If a civil engineer builds a bridge and a truck drives over it and it
collapses, he/she is liable. Similarly, when a software engineer at
Microsoft build an operating system and it spontaneously collapses (infamous
blue screen of death) they should be liable. 

I suggest Ms Tippet that next time you want to collect your bonus from
Microsoft for publically bashing the GPL, you do some basic homework first,
like actually bothering to read it, so you don't come out looking like such
a clown.

Andrew, Brisbane, Qld 

This is complete hogwash: 
"From a copyright point of view, developers are most at risk because they
lose the ability to assert ownership. But from the customer's point of view,
the warranties, indemnities, the IP ownership and all those sorts of things
are important." 

How is this any different from ANY other software license? For example a
proprietary license removes the right of ownership from the user/developer
and most mainstream proprietary licenses also limit warranties and
indemnities. 

The GPL, like EVERY other license is there to protect the IP of the author,
in this case the author has elected to give a developer the right to use
their code in another product provided they also distribute the source code
to their users. The GPL is designed so that authors can protect their IP
from commercial misuse. It's every person's right to want to maintain the
freedom of their IP. 

I find Microsoft's 'Shared Source' FAR more dangerous. In this case a
developer is allowed to see the code BUT not use it in any way. It's common
for a developer to be influenced by code they see and the danger is, after
seeing MS Code, they will be influenced to an extend where they
unconsciously re-write the MS code. They may then be liable for breaching MS
copyright. 

Open Source does not equal the GPL. If you don't want to be worried about
the GPL then don't use GPL licensed software, it's as simple as that.

Jon Eaves, Melbourne 

After reading the article "Legal traps in open source", I would encourage
people thinking of using open source software to perform their own research
rather than rely on this article for any form of decision making. 

To address just a few of the points: 

Not all open source software is GPL. Most importantly one of the products
that you mention (Apache) does not use GPL as a license, and has their own
which can be viewed at: www.apache.org/LICENSE.txt
<http://www.apache.org/LICENSE.txt>; 

The next is the implication that developers lose their right to assert
ownership, which is in fact exactly the opposite of what the GPL allows. The
ownership of the portion of software belongs to the creator, essentially
forever. 

Next is the lack of liability for products. This would have to be the most
laughable comment in the article. When was the last time that any software
product was held up for merchantable quality ? In fact most commercial EULA
disclaim all responsibility for what a software product may, or may not do.
At least with open source products there is the capacity to get somebody to
fix it. Either in-house if the experience exists, or externally if not. 

Finally, using GPL software is perfectly allowable, as is incorporating GPL
software into commercial products that are then sold. 

This particular issue is addressed well in the GPL license frequently asked
questions. This can be found here: www.gnu.org/licenses/gpl-faq.html
<http://www.gnu.org/licenses/gpl-faq.html>; 

Of course, I'm not a lawyer. I'm a software developer and this is not legal
advice, just my experiences and research from being involved with such
software for many years. I just couldn't stand to see such a one-sided fear
mongering article published without some form of comment. 

Yes, there can be issues, however the same applies with any form of 3rd
party software, closed or open.
