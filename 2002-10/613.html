when do hard drives go to heaven?

All this talk of dying hard drives worries me.  I have some old drives still
in use at home - and I wonder if hard drives just suddenly die after a few
years which seems strange or if that is really only for servers in constant
use.  I often don't turn my desktop machine off - although I wonder if my
Linux box spins down the HD?

Doesn't the Linux kernel do a sync every few minutes which makes it hard to
spin down hard drives?

I know of people who have hard drives in their home machines running for
years with no problem - so I wonder how concerned we should be over data on
old(er) drives.

> in the last 2 years I have had more scsi drives die than ide 
> (We had a bad
> batch from the IBM plant in Hungary), but even ignoring this 
> distortion, I
> have to say that I have not had an ide drive die at anything 
> like around a
> year after install. Years (2-5), yes. But not all ide drives 
> by any means.
> There's a couple in a RedHat 4.2 box I can think of that must be years
> old!
> 
> I have ide drives in mailservers (shock!) and quite a few 
> general purpose
> servers around the organisation. They work fine, and whilst 
> there _may_ be
> speed issues with more heavily laden servers than these, as a 
> general rule
> the benefits of cost and simplicity outweigh the counter 
> benefits in the
> scsi camp of speed and robustness.
> 
> Where we have a heavy-hitting database with many users, we use multi
> processors and a many-spindle scsi disk array, but the 
> benefits on a more
> simple requirement, with a lower load are just not enough to 
> justify the
> significant extra expense.
> 
> So if you're building a server that isn't going to be 
> thrashed to an inch
> of it's life, I'd use ide and use the savings on the memory, 
> processor and
> network. Even if your load increases to the point where an 
> upgrade to scsi
> is required, the old ide drives can be used elsewhere, and you're not
> throwing much away if you don't!
> 
> My 2c,
