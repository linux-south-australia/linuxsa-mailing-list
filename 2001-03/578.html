> Very odd, Stephen... Very odd indeed.
>
> May I suggest that we lay this to rest now? I think that we all know that
> there are some ISPs out there who offer unlimited bandwidth deals, and
> people who want them can use them. I think we also know that there are some
> ISPs out there that don't offer these deals, and if that is how they choose
> to do their business, then let them be. How a particular ISP decides to do
> business is their own business, and if they want to risk making a loss on
> some accounts for the purpose of gaining marketshare, or gaining accounts
> where they don't make a loss, then good on them. If their gamble doesn't pay
> off, then either they fold, or they find ways to make those accounts more
> profitable. There are no two ways around this -- as has been said
> previously, Fast, Good, Cheap... pick any two. This goes for both customers
> and providers -- providers choose what compromise they provide, gambling
> that there will be enough customers to take them up, and customers choose a
> provider that matches their needs.
>
> One thing that it would be good for us all to remember here is that there is
> no point at all in running a loss-making business, and the corollory of
> that: there's no such thing as a free lunch. Likewise, by the way, there's
> little point in working for a loss-making company, or even in doing business
> with one, especially if you require ongoing service and support. If the
> company is losing money, then at some stage, it will run out and cease to
> employ or provice service, and will cease to fulfill your needs as an
> employee or customer. Of course, in some situations, it doesn't matter so
> much if a business you are a customer of goes out of business -- for eg, if
> Shell started an all-you-can-eat petrol system, I'd use it if it saved me
> money, regardless of whether it caused the bankruptcy of Shell, because
> changing to Mobil or BP for filling up my car is trivial. However, if I were
> a major transport company, it may matter to me quite a lot, as I would have
> developed a business arrangement with Shell, due to the fact that I buy so
> much fuel, and if they were to fold, I would have to invest a lot of time
> and money in renegotiating with Mobil or BP... It's the same with data.
>
> Finally, I think some people are still missing the point about things
> costing more here than in the USA. As Mr Hackett said, it's largely due to
> geography. It's one of the costs of living in Australia, and it's
> unavoidable. Of course, the history of Telstra in Australia makes this
> situation somewhat worse, but fixing that won't solve everything.
>
> all MHO.
