Re: Security Guides

Thats where things get interesting.  I am only a undergrad here, but I
administer 6 or 7 computers.  For a miserable $10USD/hr.  But, when I
started working here a year and a half ago, I knew next to nothing about
linux/unix, but now I feel that I have gained some competency.  I take the
simple approach to security; close all the unused ports, update my
software, and lately I have been working towards only allowing encrypted
access to all of my machines.  This is a little bit harder with the SGI's
since we have budget constraints.

Thats where the University philosophy is flaky.  I am a student, so I
don't get ANY benefits, or even a decent wage (although $10 USD/hr for a
student at my university is unheard of).  I administer several
departmental servers that are responsible for streaming distance learning
classes, hosting in-development online courses, and my newest server is
now the universities largest e-commerce server, selling streams, online
courses, and conferences and their related material.

Part of the problem is the linux philosophy coming around to bite me in
the ass.  My boss is fairly fanatic about linux to the point where its
almost dangerous.  He is loathe to pay for anything related to linux,
feeling that I should be able to get it for free.  Unsurprisingly, he has
nothing to do with the system administration, and doesn't realize the time
involved.

Ahh had to vent ;)

Part of the reason I wanted to purchase the PDF from sans is that I am
constantly getting excellent information from them.
Interestingly enough, they list the 7 Top Management Errors that Lead to
Computer Security Vulnerabilities
http://www.sans.org/newlook/resources/errors.htm

Number 1?

Assign untrained people to maintain security and provide neither the
training nor the time to make it possible to do the job.

On that note, I am going to go start gathering quotes on the costs (both
monetary, and on reputation) of hack attacks.

thanks for the feedback,
corey
