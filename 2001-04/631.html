> I agree this is not very linux-specific, except that linux and BSD users
> are more likely than mac and ms users to stick their home machine on
> the end of a permanent link and set up a web server.
> 
> I went through the same thing a couple of years ago wehn I wanted
> to register my first web domain.
> Now I've got 17 domains, and I get pestered continually by all those
> search engines probing, probing, probing.....
> 
> When you create a new domain (if that's what you've done), you'll
> generally get a few offers from commercial concerns offering to
> register your site with 50,000 search engines.
> 
> But in fact, you'll find that shortly after you create a new domain,
> the search engines come and index it.
> 
> The other day, I made a configuration error in my new Apache config file,
> and as I left it overnight with an unintended virtual host in place,
> I got a complete indexing by two search engines before I woke up
> the next day.
> 
> Anyway, here's my answer to the question.
> Go to this page:
> 
> http://www.topology.org/websearch.html
> 
> And click on the items marked "Add URL".
> Some of these links may still be valid after the last 12
> months of not checking them.
> 
> Nowadays some search engines want you to pay to be indexed.
> But if you just register with a handful of free-indexing engines,
> the rest will come and find you anyway, including the search
> engines in France, China, Japan etc. etc.
> And some of them just download everything at your full link rate.
> (Not good if you have 60 MB of material!)
> 
> And then you'll need to get a bandwidth throttler to stop them
> all wasting your bandwidth.
>
