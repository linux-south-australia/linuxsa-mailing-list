:)

The price for -that- 1000 Mb is $110. The price for the next 1000 Mb is $180.

That's an average of $145 per Gb over 2000 Mb's downloaded, and it 
asymtotically approaches $0.18 per Mb for increasing total download 
counts.

The way I decided that I could cope with $110 for that 1000 Mb was on 
the presumption that at least some of the first 1500 Mb is likely to 
be local content (news server traffic, stuff from our internal 
content caches from popular web sites, etc). So that we could safely 
reduce the price on that chunk of traffic. However, I almost 
guarantee that for customers who download constently in excess of 
several thousand mb's per month, a great proportion (tending towards 
most) of that additional traffic is -external-.

So this is my way of trying to give back value for lower incremental 
cost for lower content. So there, I did listen, Evan :)

I think it's a decent compromise ahead of the potential introduction 
of more sophisticated options for rewarding access to local content. 
Once we can analyse our own customer usage patterns a bit better in 
the future (in terms of local vs non-local access) we might be able 
to do a bit more in this respect.

Simon
