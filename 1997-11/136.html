On Thu, 27 Nov 1997, Matthew Tippett wrote:

> There was the 'bliss' virus, but I don't think that was much of a 
> true virus, more of a replicating trojan.  I think we are getting
> into *word definition* territory here, so 'nuff said.

Bliss isn't what you could call a virus or a trojan. McAfee stood up as
soon as they heard about it and posted, "VirusScan can remove the bliss
virus/trojan".

However, Bliss isn't what you'd call a virus or a trojan. It is simply a
small program, just to prove that a trojan could be distributed on a UNIX
system. Quite simply, a system infected with bliss will always have the
file /tmp/.bliss and bliss can be removed with the command bliss
- --remove-me-please, something like that.

For more information about Bliss and the likes, I recommend you go to
Axel Boldt's homepage and have a look at his doucmentation on the trojan.

Its' silly to say that Linux isn't virus proof. It is ('cept for boot
sector viruses on the Intels).. But its' not trojan proof. A quick
definition may be in hand here :-

Virus -> A virus is resident in memory, and intends to do usually harm.
It stays active all of your session, and attaches a short amount of code
to (sometimes) each executable or .com file, so that they involke the
virus whenever they are executed.

Trojan -> A trojan horse is a bit of code that is trained to do something
harmful. A trojan generally can't reproduce itself (usually! -- some/many
trojans do) and is included in a program designed to do something other
than what the trojan is.

ie: There may be a trojan included with SplitPak 2.0, which deletes your
/vmlinuz.
