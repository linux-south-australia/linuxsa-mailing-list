Two issues here, scope and intent.

Scope
=====

Firstly I am not saying that there are people who try to affect large 
numbers of machines.  If it affects a small number of machines, it still will
affect them.  Most security exploits only directly affect a small number of 
people.  

These exploits are still possible on people who aren't directly affected. 

Intent
======

The intent of code is usually good.  Most companies will be trying to sell 
products.  If the product is small then they can be try to be sure the 
security aspects of their code are good.

If, however the product is large, then it will become difficult to make 
sure that all aspects are secure.  It takes just one buffer overflow to make
a app insecure.   

To exemplify this look at NT.  It is a fairly well designed product.  But 
because of the complexity of the system it is difficult to ensure that it
is secure.  There are lots of holes all over the place.  Subscribe to 
NTBUGTRAQ to find out how many. 

Some of the more influential people in security have said that NT is most 
secure when it is off (that can be said of all operating systems OTOH).

Also with a complex system sometimes backdoors are put in for debugging 
and are forgotten to be taken out before delivery.  They are not intentional
but are still bad.
