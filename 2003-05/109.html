Re: Democrats introduce legislation to support Open Source Software

Alright. This list is coming alive, I mean really alive!!  :-)  It 
already was alive, but wow! :-)

Ok, I have something unique to throw in.  People are people, right? 
 That's why I also think its important to watch what happens in Oregon, 
so that we can anticipate how it will play out in South Australia. By 
the way,  a lot of this thread echos what was said ih the recent 
committee hearing in Oregon.

To me, it is very clear that the position that the Open Source bill 
proponents have taken cannot be clearly explained. Microsoft and its 
cronies have played on words and misframed the scope of the arguement 
and as a  result can refute what is being said because representatives 
will not have a full understanding on the issue.

Folks, you have to be very ready to go in there and avoid these pitfalls.

Everyday people supported the bill, while big software companies sent 
out attornies, one of which was a puppet group of Microsoft.  It looked 
as though the opponents had a broad base of support, while only a select 
few people supported the bill.

To counter this type of appearance of mismatch, I suggest that a 
cohesive arguement be made by a large group with broad based support. 
 Whoever shows up to speak for the group ought to have a title that 
sounds important.

One thing is for sure, Stallman is right. The focus should not be on 
cost, it should be on access to government information, AKA open 
formats. Problem is that the bill was brought up mostlly out of monetary 
concerns, not civil rights.  Government access makes the strongest 
arguement, and leaves no room for rebuttal, just a change in the 
behavior of proprietary vendors. Furthermore, open standards can be set 
by a rule making body, and that's all a legislator wants to know. He 
doesn't have time to sort out all of the computer technology.

***** Highlights ******

Statements made by the proponents:

1. There is a bias against Open Source software and the bill's purpose 
is to remidy the bias.

2. Data is held for the public by the government in trust. Access to 
this data ought not be subject to vendor lock in and the resulting 
blackmail. Open standards will guard against vendor lock in and 
exploitation.

To me, this is the strongest arguement.  It poses the question, if 
Microsoft want to do business with the government, they had better play 
by open rules. The important piece of information that is not mentioned 
is that citizens must have access to that information as well.

3. Implied that only closed source software will have spyware features

4. Made blanket statements about security, almost mythological. That 
software is combed over by open source code readers and security holes 
are always fixed immediately, so fast that vendoes slow down the process. 

5. Linux' security model limits the damage that can be done by viruses. 
So, there are no viruses. Data will be absolutely secure. (Bad 
arguement. What if a Linux server gets rooted)

6. Software will be free and save the state money. (No, methinks that 
the state will have to create its own Department of Software creation)

( I agree that it will be a lower cost, but _not_ that it will be 
gratis. There will be development costs, expirimentation costs, costs to 
migrate)
