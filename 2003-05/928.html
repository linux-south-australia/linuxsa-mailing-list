> Hi Jake,
> 
> Other people have had lots of good advice, so I'll restrain
> myself to your resume.
> 
>   - The most interesting bits of your resume currently
>     have the least detail.  Tell more about the software
>     projects you have worked upon if you want to do more
>     of that work.  How maby lines of code, how many records
>     in the database, etc.
> 
>   - Show don't tell.  If you work on software then put
>     screenshots in your resume.  If there was a sales
>     brouchure put this in too.  Put in the positive
>     news articles.  For example, put in URLs for
>     the pages you did at Oceania.  Your resume should
>     be a sales package (because it is).
> 
>   - Zap the cover page.  Imagine that no-one reads beyond
>     page 2 of your resume unless they are about to hire
>     you and want some small talk in the interview.  Having
>     page 1 basically empty doesn't help sell yourself.
> 
>   - Reorder things to put what the employer wants to know
>     first.  So past employers and skills goes before schooling.
> 
>   - Employers have no idea what a "TAFE Certificate IV
>     Information Technology" is.  You'll need to tell them.
>     The TAFE course guide probably has a nice summary.
> 
>   - Net Media Solutions needs a lot less explanation.
>     You're selling to employers here, they want to hear
>     that you're gung-ho and have no life to get their
>     project done on time.  Don't disabuse them until
>     after you've got the job :-)
> 
>   - Put in comments from employers.  I assume you've got
>     references after each of your contracts and work experience
>     placements.  If not, go and get these now.
> 
>   - Zap the "leaving reason".  Employers don't want to here
>     that you are going to leave them one day.  Instead just
>     incorporate this into the text.  "At my holiday job at the
>     Mondello factory I packed and moved 20Kg bags".  As an
>     aside, list your employer as the factory, not the temping
>     agency which placed you.
> 
>   - If you have a good plain-background digital photo handy,
>     then put this on the right-hand side of the first page.
>     Some people remember faces not names.
> 
>   - The programming skills checklist is good.  Take a
>     company that wants a "PHP programmer with some C skills".
>     They'll start by making a stack of resumes that mention
>     PHP and C and discarding the rest.  You made that bit
>     easy for them.
> 
>     Now they'll want to short-list that stack.  But you haven't
>     given them any idea of the level of your PHP skills.  Are
>     you "expert", "competent", "have used", etc.  And you haven't
>     given them any objective measures to reinforce that opinion.
> 
>     Something I find works is
> 
>         Programming language    Skill      Accomplishments
> 
>                  C             Expert    Co-wrote 20,000 lines of
>                                          C code to implement TCP/IP
>                                          on IBM-compatible mainframe.
> 
>                  Perl         Laughable  Wrote a 150 line program to
>                                          strip passwords, it still
>                                          doesn't work
> 
>     Hmm.  Perhaps I'll just say "written some short utility programs" for
>     the second.
> 
>     Also list important technologies.  For example
> 
>          Apache modules    Expert   Wrote module in C to do access control
>                                     based on BGP attributes, such as a
>                                     user's ISP.
> 
>          OSPF, BGP         Expert   Contributed BGP Looking Glass extensions
>                                     to Zebra routing code.
>                                     <http://www.zebra.org/>;
> 
>     In you case this might involve aspects of PHP, such as the database
>     used.
> 
>   - Be concrete in eveything.  There's a few areas of
>     vagueness.  Eg: replace "I have been trained in several
>     different sections of the company" with "I was trained to
>     Flip Hamburgers, Greet Customers, ..."  Basically, some
>     employers think you are hiding something bad when being
>     vague, so don't do it.
> 
>   - Mention small certificates.  Driver's license (stating full or auto),
>     first aid certificate, etc.  These won't get you the
>     job but they do make people more comfortable in hiring
>     you and it's something people forget to ask about at interview.
> 
>   - Alter DOB to "Birth date: 11 June 1985, Adelaide" or whatever.
>     This addresses the citizenship question for employers which work
>     on military projects.
> 
>   - If you are in the CFS or Army Reserve or give blood or ... state
>     this in the resume. There are two employer reactions to this.
>     The ones that place you in the "not" pile you don't want to
>     work for anyway.
> 
>   - Replace all the abbreviations.  Employers are older than
>     you.  Often by a few generations.  They may not have been
>     raised in Australia.  They have no idea what "Yr R - 3"
>     means.  At least "Reception to Year 3" gives them a fighting
>     chance to mentally translate to "Kindergarten to Third Grade"
>     if that's what they need to do.
> 
>   - "Subjects for future".  Convert this from a list to prose
>     (to distinguish what has been done from what you plan to
>     do) and make it more concrete.  "Next year I plan to take
>     evening classes to obtain a Microsoft Certified Engineer (MSCE)
>     certificate".
> 
>     At the moment it's not impressive because it's way too long.
>     There's at least three years work to do all that list.  It looks
>     better to have a concrete short term plan than a wishy-washy
>     long-term plan (which isn't to say don't have long term plans,
>     but that you don't put everything into a sales pitch).
> 
>   - Put an area code on your phone number.  And its a "telephone"
>     not a "ph".  "Telephone: (08) 1234 5678".
> 
>   - If you've won anything on merit, or have passionate hobby,
>     or have written a substantial work then mention that (unless
>     it's  controversial).   Employers want to know that they are
>     hiring sucessful people.  Don't fret if you don't have
>     anything yet, these pile up as the years pass.
> 
>   - Some employers will want you to work interstate (either
>     fulltime or for periods), or travel, or work long hours
>     at critical times [eg: my answer is yes; yes; not on Thursdays].
>     Have a position on these.  Mention  them in the resume if
>     positive, leave them for the interview if not.
> 
> Best of luck,
> Glen
