On Wed, 2003-05-28 at 20:31, Richard Russell wrote:
> > Hi Richard,
> > 
> > As someone who works regularly with both FreeBSD and Debian 
> > systems, I'm
> > curious: would you mind highlighting the main problems you 
> > are finding with Debian?  
> > 
> > Is it in large part the problem of the slow Debian stable 
> > branch release cycle?
> 
> Primarily, yes.
> 
> I originally adopted Debian because I thought that it would enable me to
> keep up to date with recent software, but would remain more stable than
> RedHat (and I don't like RadHat for a variety of reasons). Evidently,
> this isn't the focus of Debian, and it seems that FreeBSD (and possibly
> gentoo) might be able to give me similar stability, but more up-to-date
> packages.
> 
> It's really frustrating to go 'apt-get php4', and then try some php
> thing, only to find that it requires php 4.3.0 or later (which was
> released in Feb of this year), and I only have 4.1.2 (which was released
> about Feb of last year). It certainly means that if I ever have a
> problem with a certain version of a program, I can't really go to the
> developers, because they tell me to use the most recent version. I also
> find that it's not entirely trivial to work out which known
> vulnerabilities I am still open to, and which have been fixed, because
> while I have apache 1.3.26, it's had numerous security patches
> backported to it, though I'm not entirely sure which ones.
> 
> Yes, I understand the reasons why debian's process is as it is -- I
> waited quite some time for Debian 3.0 to come out, and was quite happy
> with what I found at the time, but now, it feels restrictively ancient.
> I'm not criticising debian per se, I'm just deciding it's not suited to
> my needs.
> 
> I'm also aware that I can download src-debs, and compile my own debs,
> even updating them myself. However, I don't want to do this for a few
> reasons:
> 
> Firstly, Debian isn't exactly designed to make this easy. It's not that
> it's impossible, or even incredibly complex, but I can certainly say
> (from experience) that it's a more invovled process than I really want.
> 
> Secondly, avoiding getting heavily involved in individually upgrading
> packages was one of my (misguided) reasons for choosing debian in the
> first place.
> 
> Thirdly, there are other OS's (FreeBSD and gentoo in particular) which
> appear designed for this very purpose, yet may well share Debian's
> reliability and stability.
> 
> 
> The other problem that I have found is perhaps more an irritation than a
> real problem, and it could just be something that could be tuned out, or
> it could be unavoidable in any OS... Performance seems pretty average. I
> have a software RAID1 with ext3, and I find that a lot of CPU time is
> spent on doing journalling and raiding... I certainly notice a complete
> lack of responsiveness when I'm doing anything with the filesystem. The
> machine is a 700MHz Celeron with 512 Mb RAM, so it's not exactly slow...
> 
> 
> Finally, I am sick of the SysV init scripts, and while I don't go much
> on ye olde BSD style scripts, I think I like the new ones in FreeBSD 5.0
> -- they work out their own dependencies and all...
> 
> 
> rr
