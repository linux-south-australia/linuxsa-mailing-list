FYI, mail headers are lines that mail servers use to track what has
happenned to a mail message, who it was from, when it was sent, and so
on and so forth. Ones you would be most familiar with are the To:, Cc:,
From:, and Subject: headers. Here's a few specific headers from that
diff that you might recognise:

Date: Mon, 26 May 2003 11:43:02 +0930
From: Greg 'groggy' Lehey <grog@lemis.com>
To: Brian Astill <bastill@adam.com.au>
Cc: lca2004-helpers@linuxsa.org.au, David Lloyd <lloy0076@adam.com.au>
Subject: Toy MUAs (was: [lca2004-helpers] Badges for LCA)

I've got rid of a lot of other stuff there, to show you the most
relevent headers. Most of these, normal mail clients use to sort
messages and so on by.

The other stuff is occasionally useful, though ignored by most people.

The diff that Greg sent showed all the differences between his original
email and the one he got back from the mailing list. It was a "unified
context diff", which means that it has all the '+' and '-' and so on at
the start of each line, and that it shows three (or thereabouts) lines
of unchanged context before and after every change. His point was that
there was no change in the actual content of the message.

As an example of how this works, try it for yourself. Copy a text file
somewhere -- make it a reasonably large file, like your httpd.conf or
perhaps an init script -- and then make three or four small text changes
to the file. Now do this:

diff -u originalfile modifiedfile

You'll see some output that looks similar to what Greg included. I did a
simple test on my own httpd.conf:

----
richard@pogona:~/$ diff -u /etc/apache/httpd.conf httpd.conf
--- /etc/apache/httpd.conf      Sun May 25 12:22:38 2003
+++ httpd.conf  Wed May 28 01:11:15 2003
@@ -11,8 +11,9 @@

the line starting with "---" is the name and date of the first file.
the line starting with "+++" is the name and date of the first file.
lines starting with "@@" are the locations of the changes in each file 
  (distinguished by + and -), and specifying the line number of the
start
  of this section of changes, and the number of lines that are listed.
lines starting with " " are the same in both files
lines starting with "-" are in the first but not the second
lines starting with "+" are in the second but not the first

So, greg did that with two mail files -- these are the raw emails, as
KMail sees them. Depending on your mail server, they might exist as
separate files on disk, or they might actually be sections of a single
file.
