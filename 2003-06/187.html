Hi All,
    I have only skimmed this thread and didn't really pick up on what
prompted this particular part of it, but I do know a little about helping
people in need and being sued for various reasons. I am not a lawyer, but
since it is an important topic, they cover it fairly extensively in some
circles like first aid courses etc. I do hold a current Senior First Aid,
and this is how I understand any good Samaritan laws work all over
Australia.

    Firstly, you cannot be sued for negligence if you assist an unconscious
person (who may be dead or dying) who later do indeed die or recover but
with some injury like paralysis provided they are better off than if you had
left them alone and you have provided assistance as you were trained to do.
If you were attempting to assist that person and you made a genuine mistake
in that assistance that may have caused some injury you cannot be sued. If
you arrive at the scene of the accident and either choose not to assist a
person or decide that you can no longer assist that person and stop and they
die or deteriorate you cannot be sued.

    Basically what you can be sued for is if they are conscious and tell you
not to do something and you do - but as soon as they fall unconscious they
can no longer either decide for themselves or change their mind so if you
assist them, even if they told you not to previously, you now cannot be
sued. If you have been trained in first aid and deliberately do something
wrong, and you know it is wrong and something happens, i.e. death etc., you
can be sued, but if you make an honest mistake, then that is o.k. you cannot
be sued. If you are a Doctor, or an emergency/rescue worker or paramedic and
you refuse to help someone and they die etc., you can be sued, but if you
are simply a first aider, then there is no legal obligation for you to
assist (morally you may feel like crap, and someone may try to sue you by
playing on the guilt you may feel, but they cannot win unless you let them -
legally they have nothing to stand on). If you have no confidence, or are
physically or mentally exhausted and stop giving assistance once you have
started, you cannot be sued.

    Basically it comes down to if you do your best, and the person is better
off than when you found them and you don't do anything deliberately wrong,
you cannot be sued. If they were unconscious - there is not necessarily any
way to tell if they had spinal injuries for instance - if they live and are
paralysed after you saved them and all you did was helped, then they are
better off. Alive is better than dead. As a first aider, you are only
trained in simple techniques of life support, you are not expected to do the
ER tricks, you are there to simply keep someone alive if you can. You have
no legal obligation to help because you are not a Professional like a Doctor
or Paramedic. The whole point of the law being written this way was to
encourage people to assist when they can with no fear of litigation. Idiots
who sue because they can or think they can  - lose, and you as a first aider
have nothing to fear.

    Duty of Care only applies in doing what you have been trained to do. If
you have been taught CPR and you do it as you have been taught - you are
fine. If you crack a patients chest with a knife and hand pump their heart
because you saw it on Chicago Hope - look forward to prison and a huge fine.
Duty of Care also does not apply if you are physically or mentally unable to
continue until help arrives and you stop. Anyhow, I hope this has helped
clarify your thoughts. If you have any doubts, give your local first aid
trainer a call and check it out with them. As long as you do your best, you
have nothing to fear.
    Cheers!
        Andrew.
