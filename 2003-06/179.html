> In your case, the insurance of your friends swimming centre covered the bill, 
> so it would have to have been decided that your friend was acting as an 
> employee of the swimming centre when he saved that guy.  
> 
> Along this line, he was hence being paid to save that guy by the swimming 
> centre (as he was acting under employment), and hence he wasn't a good 
> samaritan and hence he couldnt rely on the defence of being a good samaritan. 
> 
> This seems a little weird, as he wasnt acting under any instructions from the 
> swimming centre to save the guy or anyone else for that matter, but from what 
> i've been learning, this doesnt have to be the case. He was employed as a 
> life saver, hence it would be reasonable to say that any act of 
> 'life-saving', whether at the swimming centre or not, was an act done under 
> employment. 
> 
> This would explain why the good samaritan act didnt apply. About whether your 
> friend should have been liable (through the swimming centre) is a totally 
> different matter - and clearly the court decided (whether correctly or not) 
> that this was the case. 
> 
> It is interesting to think that if at the time your friend wasnt employed by 
> the swimming centre, then there might well have been no liability at all as 
> the good samaritan act would have applied.
