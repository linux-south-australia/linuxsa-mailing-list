When I say "administer Unix", I mean being aboe to sit infront of any 
Unix-like machine, and have some reasonable chance of finding their way 
around... Personally, I commonly encounter Debian (3.0, 2.2), RedHat 
(anything from 6.0 on), FreeBSD (3.5 and 5.1), Solaris (8 & 9 on Sparc, 8 on

ever used the training wheels of RedHat GUIs (or the Uern /newetc -like

say they couldn't figure it out, or that they wouldn't be better than 
someone with no experience... It's entirely subjective (and hypothetical), 
but I would claim that if all they had seen was GUIs and a 
customised/unified /etc, they would be lost with a conventional /etc.

Yes, I see your point. I'm not intending to be elitist here. I'm throwing 
some realistic objections at an interesting and noble idea. It happens to be 
an idea that I like, but believe is destined to fail, at least, when 
attempted on the scale that Damien was talking about. Don't let that 
neccessarily put you or anyone else off -- I'm no expert, and even if an 
expert said it can't be done, doesn't mean it can't. 

*However*, the range of problems that Damien is trying to solve in one hit 
happens to be a range that I think is pretty much insoluble, even on a 
purely technical level. When you add the personalities of thousands of 
Linux, BSD, Unix, and software developers to that mix... wooo! Not to say 
*some* progress can't be made, even in that environment... 

As I understand it, here are Damien's requirements (this is just in terms of 
config systems, and I've re-organised it so I find it easy to understand all 
at once): 

Unified config system: 

1 Modes of editing/reading:
 1.1 -- hand-edit files
 1.2 -- (generic) GUI tools
 1.3 -- programmatic interface 

2 File Syntax
 2.1 -- human readable & writable
 2.2 -- machine readable & writable
 2.3 -- robust
 2.4 -- self-describing
 2.5 -- UI-defining
 2.6 -- powerful enough to express any possible config
 2.7 -- simple enough to express simple configs simply 

3 File tree
 3.1 -- intuitive
 3.2 -- structured 

4 Practial matters
 4.1 -- platform independant (or highly portable)
 4.2 -- extensible
 4.3 -- easily maintainable
 4.4 -- compatible with present /etc and programs 

Some of these aren't Damien's original goals -- I added some. I think 2.1, 
2.2 and 2.3 are pretty much the technical obstacles. Off the top of my head, 
I can't think of any syntaxes (syntaces? syntaxen?) that can be read and 
written by both machines and humans, without being severely restrictive on 
either, while retaining some robustness... 2.6 and 2.7 have a similar 
problem -- I can't think of many expressive syntaxen which are also simple. 

If technical solutions can be found for these (I don't claim they can't, 
only that I am not aware of anything suitable), then you're part way there. 
You then have 4.1 and 4.4 to deal with, which will be headache city (and are 
the reason why I think these problems are best attempted by a single 
distribution). Even then, you have politics and marketing... It's a tough, 
tough project, and it's one that, while I like the idea, I don't think it 
would be worth the effort...
