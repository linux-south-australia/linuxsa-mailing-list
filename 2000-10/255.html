> LVM has traditionally allowed "logical" volumes to be build out
> of individual blocks of disk (but larger than a disk block; typically
> several megs in size).  It supports both aggregate disk size (so you
> can build a "disk" that's as large as all your disks added together),
> and supports mirroring to multiple disk sets and striping.  Very funky,
> and most of the big Unices use this.  Not sure what the state of the
> art is for Linux LVM currently.

At the moment, im using LVM with the 2.4.0-test9 kernel across 4 IDE drives.
It works VERY well and is a much better system of disk managment than the
current (non-existant) system.
I have 4x2Gb partitions (2 off the 4Gb drive, and then all of the other 2Gb
drives) in one volume.
Ive then created a 128Mb logical disk (in the volume), that is in RAID0, for
my swap space.
Then the rest of the space is in linear setup in one logical disk that i then
use for /home.
The best parts of LVM are that the volumes/logical-drives can be resized on
the fly, and also that using the drives in linear, is safer than RAID-linear.
In RAID-linear is one drive goes, you loose the lot, but with LVM, only that
drives contents is lost.
