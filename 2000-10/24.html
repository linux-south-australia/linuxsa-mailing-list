>A work book has exercises with answers for you to do,

>'Kerningham and Richie' is a good example of a text

Ummm, I would have thought that lists were fundamentally related to
pointers, and that in any case, that dynamic allocation techniques are
similar in most languages. For example, I can see little difference between
dynamic allocation in Pascal, Simula 67 and C, although I must admit that I
have not programmed in Pascal or Simula 67 for a long time.

However, the biggest thing to get your mind around in C is the similarity
between pointers and arrays ... IMO

>In short buy 'Thinking in C' and stay well away from
>SAMS ie. teach yourself in 12 minutes, (That's no joke
>they actually have that book.)

Probably almost any book would do if supplemented with healthy doses of
real programming, but real programming involves quite a lot these days, and
looking at the source code for almost any open source software today will
almost overwhelm the beginner, again IMO.  This is because they are full of
#ifdefs that work their way around the differences between different
versions of UNIX and different architectures ... 

>Linux is written in C, in fact there are reports that
>C was originally written to write Unix.
