Hi...

> DHCP will be used, because its much easy to manage.

There is a tradeoff here between ease of management and ease of
configuration.  DHCP is marginally easier to manage (than the method
described below, in this situation), and slightly easier to configure
initially.  But when these users take their machines home and try to
make them work on their LANs they mostly won't be using DHCP servers,
so they'll have to figure out how fix their configuration.

For most distributions, turning off DHCP is trickier than changing the
machine's IP address (particularly for newbies).

We will certainly have less than 200 machines that need to join the
network throughout the day, so handing out IP address tickets to users
that need them will not be a hassle.

We did this last year, and I don't think we had any troubles with it.
For the sake of the installees (and the LinuxSA list as well), I
suggest we do it again.
