The rate is almost certainly determined by the number of IP
addresses you have exposed to the net, not the bit-rate
of the pipe.

For Code Red the First, I estimated various rates in the range
1 to 2 probes per hour per IP address.
But the greater "efficiency" (in the sense that Pol Pot
was "efficient") of Code Red II has led to numbers like
8 to 10 probes per IP address per hour in 203.* space.
Some reports seem to indicate that some US-dominated
/8 spaces are getting higher rates than that.
That's not surprising.
I'm observing mostly Asia-located infected hosts, and
the number of Australia-located infected hosts has been reducing
to some extent over time.
(Here are my cumulative lists:
http://www.topology.org/extra/XXXXau.txt
http://www.topology.org/extra/XXXX.txt.)

Using these rough numbers, a scan rate of 100 per second
translates to about 360,000 per hour, which would be
expected for about 40,000 IP addresses.
That's not too far from 65,535.
Is that the IP-space-width of your pipe, Glen?

I'm talking about incoming probes here.
Maybe Mark's higher rate than 100 /second is outgoing probes?

a very unpopular web site indeed (www.alankennington.com):
http://www.topology.org/extra/site/ak/usage_200108.html
Since there is virtually not real traffic here, this shows
clearly the effects of Code Red I and II as they stared up.
