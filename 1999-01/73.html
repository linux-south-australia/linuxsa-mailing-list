Along the lines that Mark suggests, it often helps in these
situations just to let people know that you're watching their
mail volumes, and that large volumes have a significant impact
on the other users.  A cron job to kick of a scan of the mail
log for long jobs and generate an email is pretty easy to set
up, and you can include some standard suggestions on reducing
mail message size.

We manually review the logs every now and then, and send our
users a reminder that large messages consume a lot of resources
and that they should, for instance, zip large word documents.
Usually that changes behavior pretty quickly, and we don't have
to spend a lot of time raising limits so that one or other user
can send a large message.  We rarely have to ask more than once
and are happy to accept that some users have a genuine need to
send large files.

Frequently the offenders are newbies who have no idea of the
problems they are causing - we had a new customer who started
receiving a daily 60 Mb data file by email.  We pointed out
that this was pretty excessive, and that they could compress
the data with pkzip (in this case a 20:1 ratio was possible!).
It turned out our new user didn't have the program to analyse
the data in any case, and were more than happy to cancel the
mail item.
