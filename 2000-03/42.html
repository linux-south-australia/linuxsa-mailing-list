> The First Amendment to the US constitution is very clear about freedom
> of the presses, and the Supreme Court has a history of interpreting
> that freedom very broadly (even if state and federal congresses haven't).
> So the Government knows it can't legally restrict a book.  So if you
> print your code out on paper and send it by snailmail out of the
> country, that's legal.
>
> If you use the 'net to do the same thing, the Government has historically
> argued that since that doesn't involve a "press", freedom of the press is
> irrelevent.  Programming, they say, is not "speech," so free speech rules
> don't apply.  Some courts have agreed with them, some have concluded that
> the academic discourse between crypto researchers who express their ideas
> in software definitely count as speech, so export should be ok.
>
> PGP 5.0 was exported from the US as a 13-volume book published by
> MIT press, which contained the complete source code in an OCR-friendly
> font with a CRC at the end of each line and the end of each page which
> could be used to error-check the process of scanning it back in.  During
> a hacker festival in the Netherlands, several hundred people took on
> the task of getting the software to compile, whereupon it was published
> on ftp servers in many other countries which don't have fucked-in-the-head
> crypto export laws.  That's how we saw the world's first legally exported
> version of PGP reach world-wide availability.
>
> Anyway, the US Govt recently relaxed its crypto export regulations.
> You can export any freely-distributable software as source code; you
> do need to send a once-off advisory of the software's URL to the US
> Commerce Dept, though.  Technically, that means software like PGP and
> the upcoming Mozilla M14 (which will have SSL) can now be legally
> exported.
