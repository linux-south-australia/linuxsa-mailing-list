Primus Telecommunications has won the race to become the first Australian
phone company to launch a commercial data service based on
high-speed asymmetric digital subscriber line (ADSL) technology.
ADSL operates over standard phone lines, theoretically  
allowing data speeds up to 140 times faster than a standard 56kbps modem.
ADSL transfers incoming information at up to 8 megabits
per second (Mbps) and sends data at about 1 Mbps. However, it requires a
special type of modem and operates only as long as a customer is within 5
km of a local telephone exchange.  

Beginning today, Primus is providing high-speed ADSL
connections to CBD customers in Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, Adelaide and
Perth, and expects to expand coverage to the rest of those metropolitan
areas by the time rival Telstra begins offering its own ADSL
service in August.

The technology has been hugely popular in countries
such as New Zealand, the US and UK, where it has been commercially
available for more than a year.

ADSL's roll-out in Australia has been delayed, in part,
by a dispute over access to local telephone exchanges which are controlled
by Telstra.

Last year the Australian Competition and Consumer   
Commission forced Telstra to allow
competing phone companies access to local exchanges and
competitors were finally able to install ADSL equipment.

Primus has wasted no time in taking advantage of the 
decision. By beating Telstra to the
punch the company will probably kick off a price war
that will ultimately drive down the
price of high-speed (or "broadband") Internet access.  

Residential subscribers will pay about $75 a month for
ADSL service, which also requires a $400 ADSL modem. This is about the
same price as high-speed cable modem services already offered by Telstra
Big Pond Advance and Optus@Home.

The Primus announcement yesterday appeared to catch
Telstra off guard. "We're in a competitive environment and we understand
that," a Telstra spokesperson said. "We will go into full roll-out from
August and we feel we will still be competitive."
