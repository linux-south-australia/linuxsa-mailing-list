>The In Formation Society series(IFS)
>
>Information Poverty
>
>by Phillip Charlier
>
>I recently read the transcript from the ABC radio science program 'The
>Slab: 'How the Internet is failing the developing world' by Subbiah
>Arunachalam. Arunachalum explains that there is an ever increasing
>decline in the ability of Third World scientists to do their work or to
>keep abreast of and participate in developments in their field because
>they lack access to the internet. In other words, people who suffer from
>information poverty are beginning to lose their jobs.
>
>Fortunately, regional Australia has a far better telecommunications
>infrastructure than those of some Lesser Developed Countries, however we
>are still far behind our metropolitan cousins in many ways. I look with
>envy at internet access advertised in the cities from $15 per month
>unlimited and can't help thinking that my peers in the information
>industries there have a comparitive advantage in terms of speed, quality
>of service and costs. I pay over $1 per hour and I can tell you that it
>really adds up when you are in the information game. As for speed, I
>might as well use my old 14.4 k modem to connect from Watsonville via
>Ravenshoe to the world wide web.
>
>I wouldn't complain but I watched the fibre optic cable being laid last
>year about 500 metres from where I am sitting now. I know it is there
>but you wouldn't know it when you plug in a modem.
>
>The problem of inadequate access is not cost, for the benefits of the
>information society are huge compared to outlay. This is what Subbiah
>Arunachalam observed about the problem in India:
>
>What is actually happening is disheartening. Government departments set
>up committees, file reports and do not act, whilst the different
>agencies in the telecom sector who have to implement and deliver
>solutions merely quarrel with one another. Indeed, this is
>characteristic of the Third World: it often takes far too much time for
>things to happen or to translate something from the realm of the
>possible to reality.  Arunachalam 1999
>
>I would say that it is characteristic of the information revolution per
>se: in many lesser - developed areas of society. The bureaucracy and
>other corporate bodies have never been in a hurry to let go of IT. They
>know that information is power and they want to be the ones collecting
>the forms, not the ones filling them in. Meanwhile in the
>telecommunications sector, the privatised carriers are fighting for
>control of the highways, not fighting for those who may turn into a
>competitor (and in a privatised world that is all of us).
>
>The irony is that it is the free flow of information that makes the
>wheel revolve and the revolution continues to gain pace. If all of the
>bureaucrats in the world put all of their red tape together into a big
>thick scarlet ribbon of rope the wheel would still break through. If the
>scarlet rope does not break they will be in even bigger trouble because
>there are not many of them left to hold on to IT. The revolutions'
>momentum increases far more rapidly than bureaucratic procedure.
>
>This is less of a problem in more urbanised areas of Australia where
>early demand and competition has created an ideal infrastructure for
>doing business in the Information Economy. People in regional and rural
>Australia, however, are used to being more dependent on government
>policy and government regulations that guarantee an adequate standard of
>service for rural and regional citizens. The bureacracy that usually
>implements these policies is vastly diminished and the remnants are
>aware of how tenuous their tenure is.
>
>This means that they won't do anything to help you unless you force them
>to.
>
>The age of the apathetic Australian is over. The evolution that comes
>with revolution ensures that the age of passively recieving filtered
>information and enjoying the bliss of not having to take responsibility
>for choosing the information recieved must end. The choice of
>information available on the internet means that every click contains a
>moral, ethical, economic, political and social question; in other words:
>'How are we going to use our time and our energy in the In Formation
>Society?' IFS...
