I sort of agree... like newsgroups, one should be free to come & go

There is need - e.g. by local resellers & consultants (is SA) - of
people (here) willing to help generate business on Linux-based
systems; I sometimes wonder if at least *some* of the periodic
"where can I get RedHat Linux x.x?" type questions are meant
to generate sales (initial & follow-on) for the local Linux reseller,
even when there are others (in Australia) who may be price-
competitive... or stock a broader range of products... we're
used to "deli's" here, so it's not so surprising...

If non-Adelaide people (especially from other lands) care to let
an Adelaide-firm get most of the air-time, e.g. rather than help-
ing to grow their own local suppliers, so be it.

For me, "Think Global, Act Local" make more sense, at least
in the long run... so, you might like to keep a foot in more than
one puddle (if local conditions preclude Linux growing into a
pond, at the moment...), just to help *your* local Linux market
grow, provided, of course that it's doing so in a competitive
manner...  ;)

BTW, why not a hierarchy of Linux info sources:

  Global:     Long-term planning (who's porting to Linux),
                   new version announcements, etc.

  Regional: Cross-Pollination (not every 'local' does everything),
                    Designing & Building new software (in small groups),
                    Telecommutor-style Consultants/Dev'rs (e.g. via I'net)

  Local:       Resellers & Consultants, HowTo connect to <local-ISP>?
                    Install-Fests (preferably, on *locally* convenient
dates,
                    altho there is a move afoot to 'march-in-step' with
these),
                    Technical/Social contacts or cooperative ventures:

                    - pooling resources to buy software or books together;

                     1. OpenBSD sells to ANYONE for 40% less when
                     just 20 buy it together.  Even a small LUG should be
                    able to get that kind of ordering quantity, as standing
                    orders for major releases of the product, i.e. rather
                    than having to manually reproduce CD-ROM's onto
                    unlabelled, mastering-blanks from slow, local burners)

                    With enough LUG interest (and some support from
                    organisations like LI), Linux could develop a similar
                    tradition, rather than watching prices almost double
                    (will "real" Linux distributions someday cost $1,000?)

                    2. O'Reilly books come cheap (e.g. from US distribu-
                    tors) if purchased in larger batches, i.e. much less
than
                    Amazon.com & FatBrain.com 1-off pricing...

                   Both are - after all - becoming "commodity products"
                   (at least, in Linux / *BSD circles); that brings prices
DOWN,
                    as anybody knows who gets to bookshops in Wash-
                    ington, DC, et al. where local high-tech communities
                    gobble up quantities of this kind of product after each
                    new release.
