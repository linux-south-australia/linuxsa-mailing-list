Re: So many acronyms...

With reference to Marks comments .... IF, you already had some degree of
obscenity-filter in place, you would not only NOT receive the original
message(s) (ie/ the RTFM email) you would also NOT receive any
government edict because they would have to specify what words are
deemed offensive by quoting such words & this would immediately cause
such the message containing the offensive word(s)  to be destroyed!

In the short term, I honestly think that the best solution is for all
ISP's to insert email filters immediately which cause all emails
originating from any address ending .gov.au to be destroyed.... this I
feel, will eradicate many of the inherent problems of net censorship!

My tuppence ha'penny's worth :)

> Depends on how it's enforced:  The OFLC's classification guidelines
> for film specify that "gratuitous" profanity warrants an R rating,
> which would be enough to ban this message (since people can read it
> without an adult verification device), so it depends on whether the
> OFLC would consider it "gratuitous".
> 
> In one sense it isn't (because it was required as a specific answer to
> a specific question), but in another sense it is (because the acronym
> is gratuitous in the first place).  The guidelines are ambiguous even
> when they're applied to film (which is why the Classification Review
> Board hasn't had a single unanimous decision in over three years: the
> censors themselves can't agree about what the rules mean!), so frankly
> I don't think we'll have any idea about whether this kind of thing
> will be banned until the OFLC actually looks at a case, which they
> aren't allowed to do until January 1 2000.
> 
> Assuming the email message would earn an R rating for gratuitous
> profanity, there are two ways in which it could be censored by the
> Government: Either someone on LinuxSA could complain to the ABA about
> this specific message, or someone on a different mailing list who
> never saw this message might complain about something completely
> different which convinces the ABA that they should distribute a
> "special access prevention notice" which specifies that the subject of
> the (completely unrelated) complaint and everything else on the
> Internet which is "substantially similar" should be blocked by ISPs.
> 
> All copies of the message would then need to be censored to remain in
> compliance with the law, even private copies held on your hard disk:
> If, for example, your mother can telnet to your machine and read a
> private archive of LinuxSA that you happen to keep on your hard disk,
> the contents of that telnet session count as "Internet Content", and
> you are defined to be an "Internet Content Host" and thus legally
> required to act upon take-down notices.  Everyone who runs any kind of
> server (pop3, ftp, telnet, http, gopher, irc, anything else which
> generates bytes on the network) would also be an ICH -- including most
> people here when they dial-in to the Internet with their Linux boxes
> running all their network daemons.
> 
> Of course, none of it might ever be an issue, depending on your ISP.
> Some (many?  most?  all?  Who knows?  Ask the ABA!) ISPs are going to
> respond to the law by implementing censorware filtering; without
> exception, all censorware works on the basis of lists of blocked words
> (the ones with URL blacklists gather the blacklist by trawling the
> 'net for naughty words like "amateur" and "root").  Since email is
> covered by the law, that'll probably be filtered too, meaning the
> message would never have reached your mailbox in the first place, so
> you wouldn't have been able to complain to the ABA about it in the
> first place.
