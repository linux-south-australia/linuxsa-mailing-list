Skeeve,

There is a standard "AOL scam" that goes along these lines:

Send an e-mail to an AOL user saying:

  From the AOL Administrators.

  You have {an e-mail attachment, pictures, ...}.

  These are too large to store in your e-mail.

  As such we have placed them at $URL

  Please go to $URL to download them.

Then $URL asks for their username and password.

The accounts thus cracked are then used to proceed to
other scams with anonymity.

There's no reason the same or similar couldn't be done to
Bigpond users.

And then there's trojans.  Sub7 is common as dirt these
days.

So it's easy to make people _think_ an ISP has been hacked
by publishing dozens of it's passwords, when really it hasn't.
It's just a way to do bad PR.

If someone published _all_ the usernames and passwords of an
ISP, _then_ you know it's been hacked (or a carrier has listened
in on the calls :-)... but even that is being hacked by the
carrier).
