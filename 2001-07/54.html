>Ian,
>
>From time to time, I've come up with solutions to
>this problem - but none have taken off, of course.
>
>So here's another idea.
>Suppose we wanted to greatly increase linux use in
>South Australia  -  not to kill of MS, but to ensure
>the survival of linux. (Remember that in the UK,
>virtually all new government services over the 
>Internet are almost exclusively available via MS software,
>and that may happen here too. And MS is getting seriously
>into the idea of them being the certification authority
>for network services, and the gatekeeper too.
>Linux could easily be left behind, and we would
>all have to install our workstations as dual boot
>or something, just to communicate.)
>
>The idea is:
>-	Every PC shop in SA is given an A4 sheet of paper
>	with instructions on how to join a mailing
>	list called, say, linux-start-SA, or something
>	to that effect.
>-	We set up a new mailing list with the purpose of
>	getting people just basically started.
>
>The new mailing list would not be a mailing list
>for peers, as linuxSA is, but rather a list with two kinds
>of people - helpers and beginners. The purpose would be simply
>to get people up and started and basically configured.
>I know that a lot of that does happen on this list,
>but we could be swamped if we had 1000 beginners coming in
>every month.
>Or maybe the linux-start-SA list should be a chat relay thing.
>That's possibly better in some ways. I don't know.
>
>I just suggest this particular idea because we wouldn't
>want to leave the linuxSA list address at every PC shop
>in South Australia - I assume.
>But I don't know either how many people would be willing
>to staff the newbie help desk fro nothing, especially when some
>people might be doing this for a living already.
>
>Just a thought in passing....
>
>Cheers,
>Alan Kennington.
