Re: SCO CEO Posts Open Letter to the Open Source Community

On Mon, Sep 22, 2003 at 12:43:57AM +0930, Alan Kennington wrote:

 > At the time, didn't the intel family of CPUs lack an MMU capability?

So did the PDP-11.

 > Until the 386, unix on the PC would have been very difficult surely.
 > Around about then must have been the 68010-based Sun-2. (My memory of
 > this is not very reliable because the earliest Sun I used was the Sun-3.)
 > The 68010 was just able to do the required full stack-save required for
 > multitasking with MMU page break interrupts, I believe. 

The early 68000's couldn't restart instructions after page-faults, correct.

(anyway:  MMU's aren't a strict requirement for UNIX -- Cray Unicos 
seems to manage without one.  They certainly weren't a requirement for
ancient UNIX, which predated the widespread development of MMUs.  They
are a requirement for virtual memory and memory protection in the VAX
model that's used by most UNIXen today, but the PDP-11 memory management
model used different processor features to give each process an address
space -- e.g., segmentation, which Intel 80x86 processors have historically
been disappointingly good at)

 > I don't think the Intel family had this kind of full-stack-save and
 > MMU support in the 80186 and 80286, which is why linux requires a 386.

Xenix seemed to cope with the 286 architecture...
