The lesson I draw from this is that there are:

13 root nameservers.
13 .com/.net nameservers.
2 .org nameservers.

That's new to me. Weren't .com and .net once on the same physical servers
as the root domain?

The .com/.net servers don't seem to be synonyms for the root nameservers.
So I guess what's happening then is that not only does Verisign have
_control_ over the .net/.com servers; it also has physical possession of
the separate .net/.com servers - into which they have inserted the
notorious typo-squat wildcard.

And what you're saying is that Verisign have physical control over:

2	root nameservers
13	.com/.net nameservers.

The other 11 root nameservers being farmed out to others....

So now when I rent a name in .com/.net from my name registrar, they
send the request and some of my money to Verisign, and they put the name in
the .com/.net servers under their control, whereas for .org names,
the request and money go to the people who have recently taken over the
.org server responsibility and control.

--------------------
So a conclusion from this for the original query would be that
buying a .org domain instead of .com/.net will:

-	result in less money going to Verisign, and
-	result in no typo-squat behaviour if the .org name is mistyped.

These are not totally compelling reasons to avoid .com/.net names though.

But then there's the issue of retailing - i.e. the registrars.
If you register a .com/.net name with Verisign as a registrar, then
they get _all_ your money, not just a few dollars of it.
So when people have been talking in slashdot etc. about taking their
business away from Verisign, they are talking about Verisign the registrar
(i.e. retailer), not Verisign the .com/.net controller.

Thanks in advance to people who do "know better" for any corrections to my
"random uninformed conjectures".
