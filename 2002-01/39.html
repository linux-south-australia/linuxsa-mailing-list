On Sun, 6 Jan 2002 08:48, Simon Hackett wrote:
> If you spool your email into your home linux box, you would be able
> to read messages as they arrived on your server. This makes your
> linux box the end point for the SMTP transmissions to you, rather
> than your ISP's smtp server being that end point. You would then read
> messages from your own local machine via PoP or imap over your local
> network, or via direct filesystem access on the local machine.
>
> You'd also want a domain name so you can associate an MX record into
> your machine with it. But since your own domain name makes you a much
> cooler person, its easily worth the $$ :)
>
> You can then run as many email destinations on your system as you
> like because you've become your own Internet mail server.
>
> [You can also experience the joys of needing to configure it to block
> spam relaying (which, fortunately, is on by default in decent mail
> servers now) - but this is linuxsa - you wanted to learn more about
> linux, didn't you? :) ]
>
> Your machine can be an SMTP end point if it is on a permanent link
> (its trivial this way). That can be via modem, or ISDN, or ADSL, or
> whatever.
>
> Good ISP's can also offer you the capability to have a permanent IP
> address associated with your dial account, and to have your email
> spool into your box whenever you dial up, either automatically
> (auto-detecting your presence) or triggered by you (via an SMTP ETRN
> command).
>
> The point here is that these things all move the SMTP end-point right
> in to your own machine. So as each message arrives in your system, it
> turns up at the back of /var/mail/yournamehere and you can read it
> then - while other messages are still turning up.
>
> Take the opportunity to swap from bigpond to a real ISP while you're at it
> :)
>
> If this is all too complicated, here are two simpler alternatives:
>
> 1) Some mail clients (Eudora, for instance) let you tell them to only
> download the first <x> kilobytes of each message via PoP, and if you
> want the rest later, you manually trigger a subsequent 'pull' of the
> rest of the message. That allows a rapid assimilation of what mail is
> there, without the download delays.
>
> It is, however, ultimately a pretty annoying way to work (IMHO).
>
> 2) Just get a faster Internet link, so the time to download 300
> messages becomes trivial (ADSL comes to mind).
>
> Cheers,
> Simon
>
> p.s. of course, the really nifty approach is imap rather than pop.
> But imap is very much an 'on-line' protocol, especially if you want
> to read headers only now, and pull messages 'on-demand' as you read
> them. And no rational ISP will support you using imap[*], you'll want
> to run this on your own linux box, which means engaging one of the
> options noted above anyway, at which point all the mail downloads to
> your machine while you sleep anyway and its already on your local
> hard drive...
>
> [*] Because imap leaves all the mail on the server, all the time.
> Which is a blessing if it is -your- hard disk, and a curse (for your
> ISP) if it was theirs...rapidly becomes untenable in the latter case.
