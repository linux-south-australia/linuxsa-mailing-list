Re: Blocking Spammers.

Hi,

yes, having an ISP that blocks known open relays is a good method.

Having postfix or whatever your MTA is, check for open relays before
accepting email, is a similar thing, only more time consuming. Although if
you build up a "whitelist" of known-good sources it'd be ok.
I think Anthony mentioned that method to me.. can't remember the details,
but although it seems crazy to start with, after a while it makes sense..
its probably the only way to avoid spam that will actually work all the
time.

As for ricochet, you can configure it in various different ways -- i'm sure
you can get it to always post a copy of the spam source details to spamcop
or whatever.. Let us know how you go!

TC

On Mon, 21 Jan 2002 branko@senet.com.au wrote:

> Here's what I have in place:
>
> 1. Select an ISP that uses the spam "Blacklist", that way you don't
> even have to download the rubbish.
> 2. For posting to usenet (the internet news system), I have my reader
> set to a bogus reply address. VERY IMPORTANT - a major source of spam.
> 3. For surfing the net, I have given netscape a bogus username plus I
> use an account on my machine that does not match anything at my ISP.
> 4. Subscribe only to trustable mailing lists.
> 5. Where I subscribe to a less than trustworthy mailing list, I'm
> using one of the free e-mail aliases provided by my ISP, if that were
> to fall into the hands of the spammers, I would just blow it away and
> subscribe again with a new one.
>
> ... and some observations
> I get very few spams, perhaps one per month. Its not clear which of
> the above is working but I suspect that it is the first item. If so, I
> will never know to what extent the remaining strategies contribute.
> So far, I haven't had to use item number 5.
>
> Thanks Toby, I like the sound of ricochet (no pun intended). As my
> strategy appears to work due to the blacklist system, I feel that I
