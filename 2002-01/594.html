> 27/01/2002 2:59:27 PM, Adam Hawes <adam_hawes@dingoblue.net.au> wrote:
> 
> >> My usual thoughts were Lyx & Klyx although I'm not sure if they are robust enough to manage 1000+ pages.
> >
> >I have found that LaTeX is pretty robust no matter how many pages.  Most
> >We have quite a few people using it at Uni to write their final thesis
> >dissertation (which gets large, and cumbersome, and full of images).
> >
> >Lyx (and Klyx) are really just front-ends to LaTeX anyway - they use
> >their own file format, but they convert to a tex file for processing.
> >
> >Is your friend highly computer savvy or is your friend one of the "point
> >and click, oh shit, it's done something different to normal I'd better
> >call an expert" kind of person?
> >
> >If your friend falls closer to the latter category, I'd recommend
> >steering clear of Lyx and/or LaTeX.  Staroffice or Word perfect (or
> >Abiword or KOffice) may be better alternatives.  They provide a much
> >more "user-friendly" interface to the uninitiated user.  Staroffice is
> >pretty stable with large documents if you break the document into
> >chapters and store each in a separate file.  That's not too hard to do
> >once you get used to it either.
> >
> >If your friend is closer to the former category then I'd recommend going
> >straight to LaTeX source. It's not hard to learn if you have a real task
> >for it.  It's available on Unix and Windows (and just about everything
> >in between), and there's squidloads of information available to help you
> >out. I say your friend needs to be more experienced, as TeX (of any
> >variety) outputs its build errors in text form and sometimes they're
> >worse than the output of a compiler when you need to track down a source
> >error.
> >
> >If your friend falls in the middle, he can try LyX of Klyx. I tried LyX
> >once and it looked OK, but it wasn't exactly fast (execution speed), and
> >it didn't seem overly stable - I crashed it by trying to display a
> >simple one page file as DVI. I can't speak for Klyx. The interface is a
> >little clunky compared to the more polished word processors too.
> >
> >You don't say exactly what your friend is trying to do though - does he
> >have large text documents, or does he use lots of graphics and tricky
> >formatting everywhere?  These things also help to decide what software
> >to select.
> >
> >Hope this has been of some help.
