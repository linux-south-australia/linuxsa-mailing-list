Re: how to move kde without breaking it!

On Wed, 3 Feb 1999, Alex Garner wrote:

> At 12:02 3/02/99 , you wrote:
> >hi..we have a hdd with / nearly full....(78%) capacity, which is a pain
> >when installing staroffice..ie, we cant. we allocated 106 mb to it,
> >after reading the red hat install book,
> >but now, after installing kde (which we love), it is nearly full, so how
> >do i move the kde default dirs,(/opt/kde), to /usr/opt/kde/, without
> >having to reinstall?
> >
> >this is one of the probs i have found with rpm installs..little path
> >control...(when done through graphics managers, such as kpackage or
> >glint)
> 
> You shouldn't change the paths that programmes install to. They are the way
> they are becuase many many people have come before you and worked out the
> best way to do it.

C'mon this *nix. If you can't change the paths it's because someone hasn't
thought about it too hard. One of the reasons most packages support
configurable paths is so that different users can have different
configurations. And sometimes more than just a few config files are
involved. There's no "best" way. There is just the current way. Tomorrow
will always have a better way somewhere.  

> 
> What I would suggest is not havein /opt off the root. You can either
> install a new disk and tar up /opt, move it over to the new disk and edit
> /etc/fstab to sho that /opt is now on a new drive, OR you could even move
> the stuff to /usr (sounds like you have more space there) and make /opt a
> symbolic link to /usr/opt.
> 
> >i am starting to look for .tgz files in preference now:)
> 
> rpm's are lovely. They're even better if you get into the habit of using
> 'rpm' and trying a few of the many options.
> 
> The best rule I've found is don't change things that aren't broken. /opt
> exists for a reason and you're going to break many things by moving it to
> /usr. If kDE was meant to go into /usr/something it would be there now!
> 
I haven't used kde yet but I don't believe  it's fixed that tightly to a
location. Unless we've suddenly entered the world of M$ and from now on
the software makers are going to dictate how we arrange our machines.
