Re: Outsiders

Yeah. The brick hitting the foot will actually experience _less_ shock
than the pen (assuming equal impact velocities) because the foot will
deform more, so the brick will be decelerating over a greater
distance.

Crumple zones? Bricks no, HDDs maybe...
>...Broken foot, dented case and 2 crashed hard drives for evidence
... seems to me there are two crumple zones involved here!
(BTW this does not imply lack of sympathy for broken foot... I kicked
a dent in a brick wall once and I still get twinges)

Gun barrel acceleration figures are a bit misleading. I have seen a
long-exposure night-time photograph showing the flight of a torch bulb
and two AA cells soldered together; the assembly had been launched out
of a bit of steel pipe with a charge of home-made gunpowder without
breaking either the glass or the hot filament. I agree re shock of
dropping something on the floor, but I find the counter-intuitiveness
of the situation amusing.

That said, I have dropped HDDs on the floor (OK, wooden) and not
damaged them. Makes a big difference whether they're running or not. A
couple of inches is about the limit for a running drive.

>I think you may be confusing acceleration with force here. g has unit
>(ms^-2) which gives no indication of the mass of the object. if you stop
>something suddenly, irrespective of its weight/mass it will feel a
>deceleration shock that is dependent solely on it velocity prior to impact
>and the amount of time it took to decelerate. The force of the shock will
>differ - hence the greater feeling of pain in the foot from a dropped
>brick.
>
>BTW I would guess that shocks of ~300g would be easily acheived in low
>velocity impacts because of the very short times allowed for deceleration
