> > I think Foreshew was aiming it [the article] at both developers and users.
> 
> Richard is right that the opening paragraph does make it seem like it
> was going to a warn to users as well as developers, however I still see
> no actual warning to users in the article.  I suppose that sometimes
> some of us in the unix community make spurious comments about Windows in
> order to promote unix, so we shouldn't be surprised if, on occassions,
> others level equally absurd criticisms on us.  It's important not to
> lose sight of the important warning in the article.  The GPL probably
> provides little or no protection to Australian developers.
 
This is an important issue that deserves more comment. I would be 
interested in hearing in what ways an Australian developer (say on the 
Samba team, as there are three at least, in Australia, or on the Ethereal 
team, as there is at least one very active member in Australia) could have 
legal problems with software released under the GPL.

Could they, for example, be made liable for data and/or economic loss as 
a result of some large company choosing to use a GPL'd software package?
 
> Richard is correct that the article was deficient in the paragraph
> relating to distributors, although I feel his first point is not
> necessarily correct in Australia.
> 
> > Propietary Software products have long disclaimed liability, so they 
> > are in the same camp as Open Source software.
> 
> Proprietary and GPL developers may be in the same camp in USA, but
> in other parts of the world the GPL may only give the illusion of a
> disclaimer.  I shall certainly heed Tippett's warning, and not release
> any software under GPL, or indeed anybody else's licence, until I have
> my lawyer's sign off on it.  Of course, since the GPL is not GPLed and
> it explicitly denies me the right to make changes to it, I can't modify
> it to make it work in Australia.  (I find that a fundamental hypocracy.)
> 
> 
> The issue of distributors is somewhat interesting in civil proceedings.
> When you purchase a product you form an explicit or implied contract
> with the body that sold it to you.  If you buy from a distributor there
> is no contract between you and the developer, except insofar as the
> developer imposes a licence.  If you suffer loss from a failure of the
> product you have no recourse against the developer; you must sue the
> distributor.  (Should you succeed in an action against the distributor,
> they would seek their losses from the developer, but under the terms of
> whatever contract those two parties agreed.)  This situation was made
> quite clear in the worldwide windows refund day.  All of the people who
> stampeded Microsoft were given a Pepsi, which was nice, but that's all.
> Having purchased nothing from Microsoft, they were entitled to a refund
> of the same amount: none at all.
> 
> 
> > Further, a quote in the article that I have problems with is:
> > 
> >  '"From a copyright point of view, developers are most at risk because 
> >    they lose the ability to assert ownership. But from the customer's 
> >    point of view, the warranties, indemnities, the IP ownership and all 
> >    those sorts of things are important," she said. '
> 
> That's certainly a major point of criticism from many parties, but I still
> think that asserting ownership is intended to mean, asserting all of the
> controls of ownership (hereinafter described as "asserting ownership.")
> I suppose a lawyer's mindset is one of, how can you protect yourself while
> maximimisng your return, at least half of which makes no sense when you
> GPL your product because you actually want to give away financial benefit.
> Tippett is right: you do give away the ability to assert the normal
> controls of ownership.  That's one of the points of GPL.
> 
> The risks to the developer are still of concern, so a lawyer's mindset
> is not totally irrelevant.
