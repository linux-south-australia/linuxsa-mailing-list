>>Could it be that being able to build on, from and with other work more 
>>easily is what makes Open Source potentially (seem) more innovative, 
>>with a potentially larger scope of creative input?

>We're going in circles.  This is the same as the second suggestion on
>how GPL is innovative, and the answer then, as now, is that academia
>has been doing exactly that for centuries.  For that matter, so has
>industry.
>  
>
I didn't say the GPL is innovative, I said that it can be percieved to 
allow more potential for innovation due to the open way the code can be 
worked on.

>>Can anyone think of something (anything) that is dramatically innovative 
>>at all with regards to anything?
>>    
>>
>
>Flying machines.  Steam engine.  Nuclear power.  Radar.  Laser.
>Silicon chip.  Printed circuits.  Arabic numbers.  The number zero.
>Imaginary numbers.  Symbolic logic.  Machine-portable software.  WIMP.
>Quicksort.  Alpha-beta pruning.  Electronic mail.  Shall I go on?
>  
>
They are all incremental. The ideas are not original in their own right.

Flying machines copy birds and apply aerodynamics etc... Electronic mail 
uses a new medium to implement snail mail. WIMP copies the desktop. 
Printed circuits are an improvement on wiring, which is an 
implementation of a channel. Quicksort is a development on stone 
swapping. Arabic numbers are an expansion on fingers.

While my examples might not be completely technically acurate, they do 
shed light on the point I am trying to make.

Or does this mean that expansion of, or a different way of doing, an 
existing idea is innovation?

Is discovery innovation? Where does discovery end and innovation begin?
