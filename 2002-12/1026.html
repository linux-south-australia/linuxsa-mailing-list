There have been a number of articles about OSS and business plans
lately, many of them still grappling with 'how to make money' from free
software. Redhat and Mandrake play in a game of making money, both went
through an IPO and, Redhat particularly, made a great deal of money.
They are now a 'relatively large OSS company' with all the baggage that
comes with that, eg high overheads, salaries, shareholders, fiducial
duty and so forth.

What "we" must remember is that as companies come and go, Linux does not
depart with them. Debian, *BSD, KDE GNOME, and all the hundreds of other
OSS contributors will still be alive and well thanks to the nature of
people who work with OSS and the licenses that protect the software.

Governments who are committing themselves to OSS will contribute through
contracting out development, working to make changes to software that
suits them and meets their cultural needs. The list is endless.

So don't get too worried about business ventures that fail, wall street
might not be too happy,  the headlines in the papers/main web-portals
might not be too happy, but in the end we lose little, except for some
marketing hype. The source is still with us.

And if your akin to some Linux users, don't worry about who's doing what
with Linux, as long as it working for me.

Steve D
