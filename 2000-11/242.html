On Sun, Nov 05, 2000 at 01:40:38PM +1100, Greg Lehey wrote:

 > If a "winmodem" does away
 > with this double conversion, it makes sense.  But how do they really
 > work?  What work does the CPU need to do?

Depends on the winmodem.  In some cases the CPU implements the v.90
state machine, while the hardware creates and decodes the audio signals
sent and received;  In other cases, the CPU needs to do virtually everything
(when PowerMac's were first released Apple showed off a software modem
which used the PowerPC chip as a slow DSP, and the system's built-in
audio hardware to encode and decode v.32 signalling -- You could get your
Mac on the net without buying a modem at all).

As Daryl has pointed out, there are many classes of winmodems, with
different attributes which create differing degrees of load on the host
CPU.  Some of them peg the CPU at 15 - 30 % load even when they're doing
nothing, others are relatively efficient, only requiring the CPU when
retrains, connection negotiation, etc are needed.

Uniformly, however, they all suck.  Badly.  A fantastic way to piss off
just about any ISP helpdesk person is to reassure them that the connection
problems you're having must be their fault because you paid good money for
a high-quality winmodem, so your end must be fine :-)
