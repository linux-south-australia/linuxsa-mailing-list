What most people would agree a word means in the dictionary sense and
how they would use it everyday conversation are two different things
(strict definition/effective definition).  This is English, not a
strict continental language (like Spanish or Italian, although they
have their own peculiarlarities [is that a word?  Maybe I should use
quirks?]).  Most people would read the question "Which is best" to
"Which is your favourite".  This is especially true in Australia. 
For example, ask many Australians which is "best", Ford or Holden and
you'll just get their favourite (same with political parties).

If the answerer actually takes the meaning to be the "superlative of
good", this still is a subjective question.  

Ask three people 
"Which is the best operating system?" 

and you could potentially get the three correct answers: 

"OpenBSD is the best, it is the most secure."
"BeOS is the best, it's built from the ground up for multimedia
support, and is POSIX compliant."
"MS Windows is the best, it is compatible with 85% of other PC
desktops (and all my friends)." 
[Note: these are not necessarily my opinions, but merely examples].  

All three answers are correct for the individual answerers, yet all
are different.  ("Yes, we're all different.”; “I’m not.")

Quot homines, tot sententia.
