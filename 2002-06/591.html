> I will usually think of something really neat that we ought to want to 
> do and work very hard to figure out how the thing works. [God forbid if 

"ought to want to do"... hmm... IMHO, any changes that are made need
to have good business reasons for them. In most cases, this means one
of two things: They will save the business money, or they will make
the business money. If it fits into neither category, then it's
probably not worth doing. Of course, many things are long term, and
the benefits are not immediately obvious, but still, in nearly every
case, it has to be one or the other (or both). Not saying that you're
not doing that -- you may well be -- but even for yourself, the
benefits have to be greater than the cost, or the frustrations really
will get to you...

> I didn't have the wonderful help from helpful people like yourselves.

> of work, a whole lot of learning followed by disappointment that what I 
> wanted to do does not work, followed by some type of overall sucess

Yep.

When I work with Windows, which I do when I am doing desktoppy type
things, I know the set of things that I need to do (email, word
processing, web browsing, occasional sshing, and printing), and I know
that I can do all of those things with minimal excess effort. When I'm
doing those things, I don't want to be thinking about the OS, I just
want to do those things.

When I work with Linux (or Solaris, or FreeBSD), which I do when I am
doing almost anything else, particularly server and networking type
things, I know that I can do almost anything, but I will generally
have to think about it. That is OK, because the things that I need to
do (security, file serving, email servers, print servers, web servers,
VPNs, etc etc) all require thought, and so my time is not
unproductive. I know that were I to do it on Windows, I would be
incapable of doing some of these things, and would be restricted from
making the deicisions that I need to make (just point and click, and
away it goes -- don't worry, it's secure by itself), whereas on Linux,
I know that I am the one making the decisions.

Also, I'm a sysadmin -- which means that this kind of thing is my
vocation, and I don't mind doing it. If it doesn't appeal, don't do
it. :) It's really really hard to balance two vocations -- try to be
one thing, and be the best you can at that, rather than trying to do
two things badly... What I'm saying is that it's the struggle that
makes sysadmins valuable -- if it were easy, we'd be well-paid
monkeys, but we're not...

RIP is routing information protocol, at least in TCP/IP land... I'm
