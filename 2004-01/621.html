Hmm, this is a common problem. People assume that by buying
security technology and tools, they are automatically secure.

have to worry anymore.

because they don't have to provide any security. Put them in
context, and then your security is totally reliant on how those
security tools are used.

If you buy the best deadlock for your house, and then you
leave it sitting on the table, rather than installing it in the
door, can you then sue the deadlock vendor for a product failure
when you are broken into ?

I see the issue as far simpler. Why do user accounts have
passwords ? To prevent unauthorised acess.

Did Darren have permission to attempt to access accounts he was
not authorised to ? Obviously not, otherwise he wouldn't have
been taken to court.

Did he accidently access these accounts, or was there intent.
According to the story, there was intent.

If Darren broke into a house, could he use the "bored" and
"curious" excuse ? No, he would probably go to jail.

Tresspass, whether onto physical or logical property is the same.

> So, I think Darren should be treated with the same level of

> little boy who acted out of boredom, he did it for a reason,

The action of striking is not against the law. Unauthorised
tresspass is.

What's even more surprising is that Darren was going to law
school. With a background in IT, you'd think he would be more
informed or interested in knowing what was legal or not when it
comes to "hacking" computer systems.

Of course, as most people on this list are from South Australia,
he effectively broke into each and everyone of our "homes". Why ?
Because we own the State Government, and it is our data that is
