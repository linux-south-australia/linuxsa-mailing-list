 > But let's look at what the Act *actually* says about "gaining
 > access without permission" that you are referring to Mark:
 > 
 > ======================================
 > Summary Offences Act of SA Section 44:
 > Unlawful operation of computer system 
 > 
 >         44. (1) A person who, without proper authorisation, operates
 >         a restricted-access computer system is guilty of an offence. 
 >       [ ... ]
 >         (3) A computer system is a restricted-access computer system if-- 
 >         (a)   the use of a particular code of electronic impulses
 >               is necessary in order to obtain access to information
 >               stored in the system or operate the system in some other
 >               way; and 

That's the law's way of saying that you require an authenticator,
without mandating a particular *type* of authenticator.  They won't
say "password", they won't say "shared secret", they won't say, 
"proximity card" or "fingerprint reader", because to say those things
would restrict the operation of the law to computer systems which
used one of the authenticating methods they've described.

So the legislation speaks of "particular code of electronic impulses",
because that's what all of the above methods, and any other authenticator
you can imagine, will generate.

Note that a secret URL fits the bill, if "the person who is entitled
to control the use of the computer system has witheld knowledge of
the code [...], or has taken steps to restrict knowledge 
of the code [...] to a particular authorized person or class of
authorized persons."

Note that someone has "taken steps to restrict knowledge of the code"
if they've instructed an employee to secure it.  If that employee happens
to be incompetent and doesn't secure it well enough, that doesn't
mean the resulting system isn't "restricted access".

It's also still restricted access if a software bug means that some
means other than the code will permit operation of the system -- "taken
steps" is once again the key phrase.

Your reading of the law would enable anyone who was ever prosecuted
under it to defend themselves by saying, "It wasn't restricted access.
There was a bug in it which allowed me in without the password, therefore
I didn't need a particular code of electronic impulses at all!"  That
defense is highly likely to be rejected.

A particular code of electronic impulses;  Not a password.  The
Act is broader than you make it out to be.

 > Yes it does Mark, stop using the Act and read it for a moment,
 > see 44 (2) (a) and (b) of the act, the "intent" has to be there
 > and be proven, otherwise no jail sunshine.  No intent - no jail.

You're misreading "intent" too.  The law doesn't care whether you
intend to do damage, or intend to profit, or whatever.

It cares whether you intend to do whatever the Act says is illegal.

If you do it accidentally, you don't get prosecuted.  "Whoops, I 
didn't mean to add that root account;  I accidentally slipped and
hit F1, which triggered my `add root account' macro" might be a
reasonable defense.

But if you deliberately edited a password file to add a root account,
it doesn't matter *why* you did it.  You've intentionally done something
that is illegal;  Therefore the law sees intent.
 
 > And rightly so they be prosecuted - if they intentionally do it

 > actually read the Act.

There's an Act called the Acts Interpretation Act which defines a
heap of concepts and terms which are used in other Acts of parliament.
You really need to read that to come to terms with issues like "intent",
"knowingly", and other necessary prerequisites for the commission of a 
crime.  I don't think those words mean what you think they mean.
