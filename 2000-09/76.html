> So basically what is a DOS attack and how does it work?  

Robyn,

As I understand it, the generic DOS attack is an overwhelming
bombardment with bogus traffic with a view to denying the
host's services to the intended users.
That's simple enough.
But the actual _means_ of denying service could be quite broad.
Any attack involving continuous bombardment would be a DOS attack,
whereas breaking through the machine's defences to take control
of the machine is not.

Sorry I can't tell you anything non-obvious.

The main difficulty with DOS attacks is their cowardly nature.
They are almost always anonymized in that IPv4 has a
functionality deficit in not enforcing the source IP addresses
on all packets to be correct. 
Cisco routers (about 65% of all routers?) do not enforce this.
So Cisco can justifiably be blamed for all the world's DOS attacks.
If all routers verfied that IP packet source addresses were in the
subnet (or subnets) from which they came, then all DOS attacks
could be rapidly traced to source. The source would have been hacked,
but the evidence would be around of where they were hacked from.
Remember that two-way comms require [essentially] correct source
IP addresses.
Direct hacks are less cowardly than DOS attacks. They always
give away the source of the attack.
That's the thinking behind the "honey-pot" methodology, where
a security firm makes weak machines available so as to observe
hackers at work.

Just as we can blame e-mail worms on MS for writing shoddy software,
so we can blame DOS attacks on Cisco.
But it's too technical to explain to the media.
So this idea won't take root any time soon.

Well, I'm probably the least of the experts.
So I can't pontificate on this subject.

I've never tried any security tool such as freesco.
Never heard of it.

After 18 months of 20 hours a week (average) trying to
work out how to defend a 486 with redhat 5.2 (?) containing
linux kernel 2.0.36, connected to a 33k modem, I still
don't really have a clue.

All I do is update software when a serious bug is found
(e.g. sendmail, ftp, linux kernel 2.2.x [x < 16]),
and check the logs every day -- /var/log/messages mostly,
and filter out stuff that looks dodgy (using ipfwadm).

Also very advisable is to run "tcpdump" continuously
as "tcpdump -i ppp0" all day so that when your firewall
is triggered by something, you can check the whole session
to see what was done.
As far as I know there is no "1334 r00t k14" (or whatever
the sub-teens call it) which fiddles with tcpdump log files.
So that's your best record of any attack.

I also keep a list http://www.topology.org/sec.html
of links to security sites which give really good info
on security.
But I think it's pretty poor that a home user has to waste such
a huge amount of time on security when all they want
to do is get on with their work.
I've lost many tens of thousands of dollars worth of
productivity due to the security problems.
I wouldn't do it if it weren't educational.
But after 18 months, it does get a bit boring.
It's like trying to get to sleep when there is a
brigade of burglars congregating in your garden every night.
