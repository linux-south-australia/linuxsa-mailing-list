>Remember that simplicity is just that. If you want secure, don't go
>looking for anything that is as simple as one click for security...also
>keep in mind that I haven't yet come across a MS solution that is secure
>without having to buy 3rd party software to correct security holes in the

>
>> Sorry, but there is obviously a lack of knowledge on my part and I doubt
>> I'm too different from a lot of naieve users.
>
>A.K. spends a lot of time concerning security, but one thing you also need
>to understand is that Alan opted for the most insecure method of doing a
>firewall: Default was to allow stuff in, then he blocks out stuff that he
>thinks is a problem.
>If you start from a deny everything perspective, then allow back in the
>stuff that you want ppl to be able to get at, then you are MUCH better off
>to begin with. My gateway/mail/dns server has only 4 ports open, and only
>2 are accessable to the internet in general (DNS and SMTP)
>
>> Surely there is a NICE way of securing what you have to avoid these
>> exploits without resorting to archaic console commands. Or without the need
>> for introducing a firewall box etc. 
>
>I'd start by working through /etc/inetd.conf, then set /etc/hosts.deny and
>/etc/hosts.allow to batten down everything that you do not explicity
>require.
>If you are not on a permanent connection then you are fairly safe with
>just the simplistic security above. On a 24x7 connection??? Then it is
>well worth the effort getting a 486 as a firewall...I have had not one
>person get into my system yet (I do the same as A.K. and keep tcpdump logs
>of everything & also have tripwire running to check that noone screws with
>my system.
>
>Or look on www.freshmeat.net under security tools for firewall scripts
>that take care of lots of the yukky details. But make sure you check
>carefully what they are doing!
