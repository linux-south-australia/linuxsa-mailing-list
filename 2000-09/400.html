If you look at the shell script, you will find that it checks to see if
/tmp is writable (which it has to be) and is lacking the NOSUID and NOEXEC
flags when mounted.

It is, IMO, good security practice on any system with users, or on an
Internet connected system to mount /tmp and /usr/tmp with NOSUID, and
probably also /home.

This removes a whole class of vulnerabilities.
