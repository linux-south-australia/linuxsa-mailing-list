> Robyn,
>
> "ssh" or "openssh" is a safe alternative to "telnet".
> It's like the SSL of the telnet world.
> I think it originated in the BSD world.
> The idea is that it uses challenge-response
> authentication instead of sending passwords in
> clear over the net.
> Therefore it is (fairly) immune to replay attacks.
> The "ssh" documentation gives a description of how
> they defeat various categories of attacks.
>
> AT present, I keep connections open between machines on
> two different sites, and I always use "ssh".
> In fact, I turn off "telnet" completely on my most
> important site, just incase I forget...
>
> In this way, I can log in from a company overseas to my
> home computer over the net, without the company I contract
> for logging my passwords.
> The software for ssh is at http://www.openssh.com/.
> Beware of openssh.org, which is a cybersquatter site.
>
> Recently, someone in the States gave me an account on his
> BSD machine. And he put a normal password in the system,
> and I was able to use SSH in the normal way, using this
> password.
> But there's another useful mode of SSH which bypasses the
> usual mechanisms and uses some other sort of cryptography
> with encrypted public/private keys etc.
> It's all in the documentation anyway.
> It's all very technical...
>
> Cheers,
> Alan Kennington.
