> 4.  It also seems that in Greek, the "u" (upsilon) is translated
> into 'y", for whatever reason I cannot fathom.  Perhaps some Greek
> scholars could enlighten us on that point.  I am aware a Capital "u"
> looks like a "y".

It's simple.  The Greek "Y" (ypsilon) is pronounced like a Y.  Ask any
modern Greek.  Lower case wasn't introduced into Greek until later, so
the fact that the lower case letter looks like a u is not very
significant.  In classical times, the Latin U was derived from Greek Y
early on, and Y was derived later after the pronunciation had changed.
