Re: How: mv x.tom x.dick x.harry -> y.tom y.dick y.harry ?

David Drury wrote:

> > >Question: wouldn't the command `echo $f | cut -f2 -d\.` return a
> > >newline character as part of the string?  So why isn't the
> > >new filename "y.tom\n" for example?  Why does the "\n" disappear?

It's stripped out of the backticks, because it's part of the IFS
("Internal Field Separator", according to bash man page) envariable.

> Hmm, this is someting I frequently see requested, and I think I've seen
> before on the list. Its the one command that DOS does that I really wish
> *nix did. I'm surprised a GNU clommand line utility hasn't been written to
> achieve this. Anyone know why ?

Probably because the shell attempts to expand wildcards in parameters
before passing them into commands, so "mv x.* y.*" may or may not have
the effect you want (especially if there are files lying around starting
with "y.").  Then you'll have people complaining about _that_, then
pretty soon it leads to having [LinuxSA] stamped in your subject lines[1].

To answer the original question, I usually do something like (in a
Bourne shell independant kind of way):

for file in x.*; do
  mv $file `echo $file | sed 's/^x/y/'`
done
