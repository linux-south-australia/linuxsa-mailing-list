Mike Andrew wrote:

> On Sun, 02 Jul 2000, Robyn Manning wrote:
> > Hi Guys
> > 
> > This is only an observation and open to correction if need be.
> > 
> > How can anyone say they put their swap partition in the middle of an ide 
> > hard drive? There are only a couple of platters in them and a maximum of 
> > 4 heads. This would max out the number of cylinders to a couple of 
> > thousand. They use translation of some description and have more 
> > 'logical' heads and less 'logical' cylinders.
> 
> The physical geometry of any drive, Scsi, or IDE, is impossible to establish.
> The C/H/S geometry used in addressing schemes bears no relationship to reality.
> 
> For instance, drives have many more sectors out the 'outside' of the platter
> than the inside, making a mockery of 64 sectors per track.
> 
> At one point manufacturers placed the beginning of the drive viz 0/0/0 in the
> CENTRE. This, because OS's like windows placed their most accessed area, the
> directories, at the 'beginning' of the drive, and hence some speed improvements
> occurred exponentially to the hype.
> 
> the 'middle' of the drive, as per your question would, obviously, be the logical
> place to plunk the most accessed area. Swap? Perhaps, but that depends on
> whether you have a lot of ram memory (in which case, swap aint necessarily used
> at all). Bottom line, neither you nor I know where the 'middle' of the drive
> is, it changes on each model from the same oem.
> 
> The 2nd part of your question presumes that 4 heads aint sufficient, That's
> fine, but that has to be tempered by the real size of the sectors per track
> under those heads. Again, neither you nor i know. As for heads, well ok, many
> drives only have four some only use two, but the drive itself can report it has
> 256 heads if the oem feels like it's a good thing on Wednesday. No, I am not
> talking about the bios reporting 256 heads, the drive, via it's ide / scsi
> interface can lie like hell if the oem feels it's a good thing (tm).
> 
> finally, you are presuming a head only reads one data track. firstly,
> physically, you can have multi cored heads (generally separated by 10 or more
> track widths). Secondly, depending on the technology used, the coil head can
> read different density layers , and / or different recording formats on the
> same track! (IBM)
> 
> As if this weren't enough, it is the oem who decides the interleaving factor of
> his drive. Logical sector n+1 is inevetibly not next to logical sector n. It is
> somewhere further down the track (pun intended) depending on expected delivery
> (bandwidth). And, track n+1 is rarely adjacent to track n (as per the multi
> coils above).
> 
> To cut this long story short, second guessing the physical makeup of any modern
> hard disk is an excercise in futility and any software that 'fine tunes' as
> such will break on the next model from the very same oem.
> 
> None of this is scsi vs ide (atapi) related, they both come out of the same
> machine, the interface changes to suit the order. So much so, that the only
> difference between current 8 10 and 13 gig drives is the definition placed in
> the microchips on the drive. An 8gig drive IS a fully functional 13gig but
> crippled and sold as is because there's a market out there for a 'cheaper'
> alternative!
