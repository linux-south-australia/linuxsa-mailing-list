Depends on your needs. I guess there are choices to be made in 2 areas:

  How will you become proficient at document design?
    ie. So you know what design you need to achieve to suit the job.

  What tools will you use to make that design a reality.

Of course, if you are 'in the habit', you will have made these choices already...
:)

If the documents are one-off's, I would suggest a good page layout program from
the likes of Adobe. As you have already pointed out, this could become tedious if
it was a book such as yours where new editions are quite likely - in that case
perhaps framemaker or something like what you are doing. You are comfortable with
groff etc, (and that is fine), but I can't see a non-technical person taking to it
in quite the same way.

> 
> > One of the gotcha's is not the tool being used at all, it's the
> > ability of the user to keep the formatting sane. An idiot editor's
> > output will be crap regardless of the tools being used. (no grog, I
> > am not talking about you!)
> 
> There are plenty of ways a program could help promote good
> typesetting.  Style sheets, for example.  The problems we're seeing
> with OpenOffice, "Word" and Co. is that they don't offer enough help
> even for experienced users.

Hmm. Not sure I agree with that. If I was experienced, I'd want the editor to 'get
out of the way' so that I could format my document with the least amount of
hassle. Inexperienced users tend to pick an existing template and work from there
- perhaps by the time they have some experience they have created a pretty good
library of templates to suit their style. Generally, the WP's don't get into the
same degree of control of typesetting the text as a 'proper' page layout system. 

>
