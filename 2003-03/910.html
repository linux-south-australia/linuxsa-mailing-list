On Monday, 17 March 2003 at 14:31:36 +1030, Michael Firkins wrote:
> * David Lloyd <dlloyd@microbits.com.au> [2003-03-17 12:39]:
>>
>> [and correct typesetting is out of the ordinary]
>
> Yes. Unless you are in the habit of writing lengthy documents, and
> preparing them for printing yourself, the learning curve is too
> steep - in fact for one job, it's probably better to pay someone
> else to do it. (the typesetting)

And if you are in the habit?

> One of the gotcha's is not the tool being used at all, it's the
> ability of the user to keep the formatting sane. An idiot editor's
> output will be crap regardless of the tools being used. (no grog, I
> am not talking about you!)

There are plenty of ways a program could help promote good
typesetting.  Style sheets, for example.  The problems we're seeing
with OpenOffice, "Word" and Co. is that they don't offer enough help
even for experienced users.

> The other issue is that for a one-off production (like a thesis)
> there isn't neccesarily time or resources to go through the learning
> curve to typeset the text 'properly'. A good approach is to use an
> effective, but not great WP tool - AbiWord is a good example, we did
> it on a Mac with Clarisworks, and make sure to save the final
> version as a pdf for posterity's sake. Regular lengthy document
> authors will need something better.

A better approach is to use an effective and great WP tool.  As you
observer, it doesn't guarantee good results, but it makes things
easier.

> My experiences in this area with Word are not good, once the
> document size gets large, it kills the ability to edit it. We had
> some consultants at work a couple of years ago who managed to
> assemble a 14mb word doc that they couldn't open again!

I think this has more to say about "Word" than it does about the
consultants.  And yes, I've heard similar stories.
