You are right about the power output.  If I use a mast - I *may* look at a
*small* amplifier to compensate for coax loss - but you appear to be saying
that all this is completely un-necessary with a good card, good cable (and
short distance) and good aerial - preferably directional for community
networking.

Theoretically - if one can get a decent bit rate up to say 2kms - that
should be fine.  What if a dish is used?  No different to a directional
antennae I suppose.  2.4GHz is approaching microwave frequencies right?  So
some sort of dish should be possible to make it ultra-directional and less
likely to interfere with other nearby networks.

It's a novel concept that one even needs to consider that their antennae is
not *so* good that it exceeds legal restrictions..  I better keep those
Pringles cans under control..

On a side note - is it worth experimenting with lasers?  Theoretically - it
would be like open fibre - but extremely prone to the beam being blocked by
birds, low lying cloud, humidity, etc (well 2.4GHz microwaves would also be
affected by humidity I would think).

I was thinking that lasers are obviously good for directionality and for bit
rate.  I am not sure how expensive or hard it would be to set up.  I don't
know if there are some fault tolerant media converters out there - and plug
in a laser instead of a fibre cable.  I imagine one could get some really
good distances like this too.  Would be REAL pain to set up and align
correctly though..

How much higher in frequency is low infra-red compared to a 5GHz anyway?  It
seems radio technology is approaching the frequencies of light?  Could be
bad news if it takes on the properties of light - having to yell at the kids
for standing in the way of the wireless NIC.

If the power amps are designed for wireless service and are type
approved,
they are legal... BUT, only as long as the total effected radiated power
(EIRP) is less than 4 watts.  So, if you have a 24dBi dish fed from one
of these amps, you're well into illegal territory, but if instead,
you're feeding a 10dBi omnidirectional, a 400mW amp would put you at
maximum legal power.



.....and a later post ......



EIRP (dBW) = Tx power (dBW) + antenna gain (dBi) - coax losses (dB)

Simple as that


So, if you have a 1 watt transmitter into a 20dBi gain antenna and 3dB
of antenna loss, then the EIRP is...

0 + 20 - 3, = 17 dBW, or 50W.
FYP, 4W = +6 dBW, to save having to convert back to linear values.

Also, if antenna gain happens to be expressed in dBd, add 2.14 to get
dBi (doubt you'll see this, but it can happen).
