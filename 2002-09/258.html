I'm no expert on parliamentary procedure but at Question Time it seems that
anybody can ask anybody else any damned thing.  Linux. Linux.  The PM doesn't
appear to be any different in that he can be asked questions just like anyone
else.

Questions can be asked as "Questions without Notice" or "Questions with
Notice".  As I understand it, the member can choose to answer the Question
without Notice there and then, or refer it to his/her department or otherwise
seek the answers to provide to parliament at the next session.

Questions come in 3 main divisions:

1) attempt to trip up or embarass some member of the opposite party, or
hopefully the entire opposite party- very common technique used by members
from the opposition benches.
2) ask a question of somebody in your own party, the answer to which is
probably known by the questioner, in an effort to make your own party look
good - common method used by party that holds power.
3) ask a question of the opposite party, the answer to which you do not know
or to which you would actually like to know the answer - rare, almost never
happens. ;)

Commonly used strategies to answer:
1) Don't answer it: just talk about any damned thing, "beat around the bush"
for a while, use the words "Mr Speaker" a lot, and just keep filling in time
until you are told by the Speaker (that's the moderator of our parliament,
who sits in the big chair), either:
a) "The time for answering the question has expired.  The member will resume
his seat", or
b) "The member will come to the question" (or similar words).
2) Don't answer it.
3) Don't answer it.
