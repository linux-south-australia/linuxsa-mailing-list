> I think that there's the standard list of reasons that are 
> offered to most
> commercial companies. The documentation on Linux and BSD advocacy are
> probably a good place to check for things you may have missed.
> 
> Here's a couple of thoughts that have come from my own 
> personal experience:
> 
> Most gov. depts. tend to spend a huge amount on IT - a 
> significant amount of
> that would be on software licensing. IT departments are 
> having funding cut
> (like everyone else), but instead of trying to save money on software
> ransom^H^H^H^H^H^Hlicensing, these departments are forced to 
> "save" money
> elsewhere. This includes less support staff and extending the life of
> hardware (even in mission-critical applications in 
> hospitals). (Note: I'm not
> saying hardware should be upgraded every week, just that it 
> should work).
> 
> For those that absolutely, positively have to run some 
> flavour of Windows
> application, for the cost of a beefy Windows 2000 server and 
> a number of
> Terminal Services Client Access Licenses, rdesktop 
> (http://www.rdesktop.org/)
> is a godsend. It's a lot easier to manage a set number of TS 
> CALs than it is
> to manage a bunch of OEM, upgrade, client and application 
> licenses across a
> 1500+ user network.
> 
> It's probably also worth mentioning that there's no real need 
> for someone to
> pull The Big Switch and all will be free. It's a migration, 
> so you start of
> testing and trialling, then migrate some of your less 
> critical applications /
> departments and then one day you let your software vendors 
> know that you
> won't be requiring the software licenses or support contracts 
> you've had in
> the past :-).
> 
> Hope it helps,
> Matt
