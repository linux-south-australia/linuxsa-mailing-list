Slapper.B hits Australia
Kelly Mills
SEPTEMBER 23, 2002 A NEW variant of the Slapper worm has the potential to
bring down major IT infrastructure, such as the likes of Telstra or the
National Australia Bank, Internet Security Systems warned.

So far around 60 major Australian ISPs and businesses have been attacked by
the new variant worm, labelled Slapper.B, ISS principle consultant Grant
Slender said. Worldwide around 10,000 hosts have been hit.
 
Slapper.B is simply a maliciously updated version of its predecessor which
targets the Linux operating system running the Apache Web server with Open
SSL. It could also trigger unpredictable results on additional Unix
platforms.

It carries the same payload and backdoors whilst seeking to exploit a
previously disclosed vulnerability in the Secure Sockets Layer 2.0 handshake
process.

Mr Slender said that unlike Code Red and Nimda, Slapper targets
infrastructure servicing customers, such as virtual private networks (VPNs),
mail servers and dial-up services. Like most worms before them, Code Red and
Nimda simply targeted web servers. 
				
The origins of the worm have been traced back to a 21-year old hacker in the
Ukraine, Mr Slender said.
 
"One aspect of this worm is that it emails its host and all hosts it has
affected. So the potential is there for the worms to act as one device and
to turn all the worms on a target.
 
"The bandwidth generated by this could bring down major infrastructure such
as Telstra and the National Australia Bank."
 
Mr Slender said he expects there will be further variants of the worm but if
these do not appear in the next couple of weeks, the threat will "fade
away".
