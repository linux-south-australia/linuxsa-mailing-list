Dean Gawler wrote:
> 
> Well, technically IBM's JFS is far more mature. 

[snip]

> I also don't know why more people don't give it a go on Linux

Well, it doesn't have a very cool or imaginative name (or acronym for 
that matter). How could anyone be expected to go for JFS over FS's with 
cool names like XFS or RieserFS?

Personally, I'm suprised that more people don't use EXT3 for generic 
Linux workstations. The only real benefit a journaling FS brings a 
workstation's user is the data integrity bit - being able to pull the 
machine's plug out of the wall and not losing any data. Any other 
features a journaling FS has are of marginal (at best) usefullness on a 
typical workstation.

EXT3 is a lot less suprising than the other journaling FS's, it has a 
much more mature toolset and most importantly, is backwards compatible 
w/ EXT2 and hence older kernels, rescue disks, etc, etc. It makes a lot 
more sense to me on a workstation to me that any other journaling FS.

On servers the case may well be different, but all of mine use UFS (w/ 
soft-updates where appropriate), so I couldn't comment on that.. :)
