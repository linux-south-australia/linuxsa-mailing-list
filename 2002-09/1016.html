Re: RF Power output on wireless cards - ideas?

On Tue, 2002-09-24 at 13:57, Mcleod, Ian wrote:
> >From Surecom - someone told me to get cards of at least 100mw - I am not
> sure if this makes much of a difference - or if 30mw is just the standard
> output and 100mw is the exception?

Ive seen comparison tables around, that showed that in general the prism
II cards did ~100mw and the lucent ones did less. There is no real
standard (AFAIK).

> Apparently Cisco products are all 100mw?  Something to consider for wireless
> community networking - every mw counts!  But perhaps in-house a smaller
> power output is more secure (and hence more wise) anyway.

Power doesnt usually make as much difference as good co-ax and height,
but at such low powers maybe it does.
 
> Is it possible to rig up a 2.4GHz RF amplifier with directional dish to
> create substantially higher bit rates over larger distances?  The up and
> coming 54Mbit/s cards are looking good - but they operate in the 5GHz range
> - so it will be even more important to look at RF power, using a dish, and
> so on.

Yes, but not necissarily legally. I think the ACA have attempted to keep
the power down so that if you want range you need to keep your RF beam
focused and thus are less likely to interfear with others networks on
the side. You could amp up the power massively, put a huge DB aerial on
it and boot out massively, but you'd be an inconsiderate bastard who
will probably have the aca knocking on your door when they get the
complaints from all the other smaller local operators whos networks you
just stuffed up. :) 
 

Some quotes from the wireless list:

> I have just been talking to a HAM which I know about wireless 
> and using
> power amps to extend range. He seems to think that the use of 
> poweramps
> (like the 250mw and 400mw that some company who recently advertised on
> this list have) are illegal to use in Australia. Unfortunatly 
> where I am

If the power amps are designed for wireless service and are type
approved,
they are legal... BUT, only as long as the total effected radiated power
(EIRP) is less than 4 watts.  So, if you have a 24dBi dish fed from one
of these amps, you're well into illegal territory, but if instead,
you're feeding a 10dBi omnidirectional, a 400mW amp would put you at
maximum legal power.



.....and a later post ......



EIRP (dBW) = Tx power (dBW) + antenna gain (dBi) - coax losses (dB)

Simple as that


So, if you have a 1 watt transmitter into a 20dBi gain antenna and 3dB
of antenna loss, then the EIRP is...

0 + 20 - 3, = 17 dBW, or 50W.
FYP, 4W = +6 dBW, to save having to convert back to linear values.

Also, if antenna gain happens to be expressed in dBd, add 2.14 to get
dBi (doubt you'll see this, but it can happen).
