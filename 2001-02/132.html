ADSL is a train wreck internally to Telstra. Just awful. How do they 
maintain this sort of lack of planning and provisioning quality so 
consistently with every single new 'initiative' - and this is for a 
product well over 3 years late!

Anything other than dynamic IP assignment has caused us grief with 
every single service we've provisioned thus far - literally none of 
them have worked without an initial provisioning request, a long 
delay, a fault report, another long delay, denial of any problem in 
some cases, followed by it eventually being made to work.

This is actually a critical issue behind our deferral of ADSL release 
in our own name - we are just not interested in wearing Telstra's 
shocking reputation in this space by unavoidable association (which 
is why they don't have their own static IP offering yet either);

So I'm working on a combination of creative DSL CPE configuration 
overlaid on the Telstra dynamic offering, which generates a static 
address end-to-end for our customers via an interesting sort of 
sleight of hand. The customer visible outcome is an ethernet 
delivered service which delivers via static IP's.

We can do dynamic addressing right now, and that works fine (well, ok 
enough), I'm just sure that almost everyone on this list at least 
will want a static address assignment :) We might wind up launching 
dynamic ahead of static, but I don't expect a lot of takeup on it.

Unfortunately, my preferred router platform remains mysteriously 
absent from the Telstra approved list of CPE devices, so I'm spending 
happy afternoons in international phone calls right now lining up 
alternative CPE devices that can do what I need and which Telstra 
will allow me to connect, to support static addressing. I've found 
the right hardware in theory, I'm just trying to get supply organised 
now.

What I'm cooking up is just so far and away cleaner than Telstra seem 
capable of (even though we'll be doing it over their 'network'), that 
I believe it'll be worth the wait (and my apologies to our beta 
customers who are having to live with the provisioning issues above 
on the existing apology for a static offering that we're running with 
in the meantime via Telstra - this isn't the final product 
configuration).

yes, we'll be offering static IP assignments. On current indications, 
it might require a higher setup fee so we can put in a better DSL 
device, which in turn has enough smarts for us to work around 
Telstras' internal train wrecks, but otherwise it'll be very smooth

Your Telstra representative has his brain busted, FDDI has only one 
speed, 100 megabits per second (and token ring derived half duplex at 
that). So either it's 2mb/s on something else, or 100 MB/s on FDDI 
[hint: It's likely to be neither].

Also because Telstra have so dramatically speed limited what they'll 
offer to customers (directly or via wholesale structures such as we 
use), the speed achieved will be either the stated speed or nothing 
at all.

The ADSL standard Telstra is using is capable of up to 8mb/s 
downstream - and they could choose to configure the gear to 
auto-range up to that speed where possible - but they've chosen to 
speed limit it to 1.5mb/s at best.

Why? Well, I guess they're just making life easier, eh? Got to love 
them, making sure we remain behind the rest of the world as long as 
possible.

In their world, this is probably seen as an advantage - the customer 
experience is consistently mediocre for all customers who can get the 
service at all.

Telstra's problem IMHO is related to some very bad choices of 
equipment, and some just astonishingly strange choices in terms of 
how they configure it, in respect of the DSL management gear they run 
_internally_ in their network (between your house and our head end 
routers).

Telstra didn't have Alcatel 'design' anything. These are standard 
Alcatel DSLAM units, and  commonly available (though not especially 
great in this application) Shasta PPPoE PVC aggregation units. The 
level of 'design' in this network is very very close to zero.

There are a set of worlds' best practice ways in which a Telco like 
Telstra anywhere else in the world would provide a clean wholesale 
interface to the DSLAM layer in the exchanges. And then there is what 
our Worlds Best Practice local dominant Telco does. Making life 
easier... not.

They've gone to enormous trouble to configure the shasta units in a 
manner which seems purposely designed to annoy us. And, of course, 
any attempts at requesting more flexible configuration has been met 
with the usual complete lack of timely response to such requests. 
Just a case of the usual idea of Telstra responsiveness ('oh yes, we 
might implement that in about a year').
