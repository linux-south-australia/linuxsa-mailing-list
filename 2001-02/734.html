I would think that the paranoid would use if=/dev/random, and do it umpteen
times (for a large value of ump)...

However, in answer to a possible misconception that popped up earlier, if
you want to remove all traces of a virus from a drive it is sufficient to
merely destroy the filesystem. It is not neccessary to use the above methods
simply to de-virusify your disk -- even if the sequence of bytes that forms
the virus still resides on your drive, it is not accessible within your
filesystem. It would have to be a very bizzare sequence of events that would
cause this sequence of bytes to be executed. However, when one is talking
about rendering confidential data unretrievable, what you want to do is
ensure that no matter what sequence of events occurs, the data can't be
read. Normally, without actually taking the disk apart, writing 0's all over
the disk is sufficient, as the disk itself should reliably read what it has
written. But if it's really valuable data, someone may take the disk apart,
and microanalyse the platters to find traces of what value each byte
contained before the last write... It would have to be pretty valuable data
for this to be a risk though...
